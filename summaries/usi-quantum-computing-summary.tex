\subsection{Stern/Gerlach Experiment}

Measure \emph{magnetic dipole moment} of silver atoms by sending a stream of them through an \emph{inhomogeneous} magnetic field.  Each atom is deflected from the path proportionally to its dipole in the direction of the field gradient.

\subsubsection{Classical expectation}

If we imagine that the moments of the atoms point in random directions, then the classical expectation is that the deflection pattern reaches a maximum in the middle (no deflection) and then symmetrically, monotonically and continuously decreases on the sides.

\paragraph{\textbf{Classical mechanics.}}

Angular momentum (pseudovector):
\[
\vect{L} = \vect{r} \times \vect{p} = \vect{r} \times (m\,\vect{v})
\]

Torque:
\[
\vect{\tau} = \vect{r} \times \vect{F}
\]

Relationship:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tau_L}
\vect{\tau} = \frac{\dif}{\dif t}\,\vect{L}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{\textbf{Classical electrodynamics.}}

A charge $q$ moving in a circle of radius $R$ creates a current:
\[
I = \frac{q}{T}
\quad\text{where}\quad
T = \frac{2\pi R}{v}
\]

Magnetic dipole moment of a current loop:
\[
\vect{\mu} = I\,A\,\hat{n}
\quad\text{where}\quad
A = \pi R^{2}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\hat{n}\ \text{is normal to the area.}
\]

Thus
\[
\vect{\mu}
= \frac{q}{2\pi R/v}\,(\pi R^{2})\,\hat{n}
= \frac{q\,v\,R}{2}\,\hat{n}
\]

Angular momentum:
\[
\vect{L} = m\,\vect{r}\times\vect{v} = m\,R\,v\,\hat{n}
\]

Therefore (the bridge):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mu_gammaL}
\fcolorbox{red}{white}{\(\vect{\mu} = \gamma\,\vect{L}\)}
\end{equation}
with
\[
\gamma = \frac{q}{2m}
\]

\paragraph{\textbf{Magnetic dipole dynamics.}}

Torque:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tau_muB}
\vect{\tau} = \vect{\mu} \times \vect{B}
\end{equation}

Combining \eqref{eq:tau_L}, \eqref{eq:mu_gammaL} and \eqref{eq:tau_muB} gives the precession equation:
\[
\frac{\dif}{\dif t}\,\vect{L} = \gamma\bigl(\vect{L}\times\vect{B}\bigr)
\quad\text{or equivalently}\quad
\frac{\dif}{\dif t}\,\vect{\mu} = \gamma\bigl(\vect{\mu}\times\vect{B}\bigr)
\]
Hence $\frac{\dif\vect{\mu}}{\dif t}\perp\vect{\mu}$, and therefore $\lvert\vect{\mu}\rvert$ is constant (only the direction changes).

Energy:
\[
U = -\,\vect{\mu}\cdot\vect{B}
\]

Force in general is
\[
\vect{F} = \nabla\bigl(\vect{\mu}\cdot\vect{B}\bigr)
\]
and if $\vect{\mu}$ is constant and $\vect{B}\approx [0,0,B_z(z)]$, then
\[
F_{z} \approx \mu_{z}\,\frac{\partial B_{z}}{\partial z}
\]

So, as mentioned above, according to classical physics we would expect to measure
\[
\mu_{z}\in[-\lvert\vect{\mu}\rvert,\,+\lvert\vect{\mu}\rvert]
\]
i.e. a continuous range and thus a continuous vertical smear.

\subsubsection{Observation}

That is, however, not what was observed: there is no detection in (not even close to) the middle, but rather two sharp peaks at equal distances from the middle.

In quantum mechanics, $\vect{L}$ becomes an operator with three components $\hat{L}_{x},\hat{L}_{y},\hat{L}_{z}$ and they do not commute, e.g.
\[
[\hat{L}_{x},\hat{L}_{y}]
:= \hat{L}_{x}\hat{L}_{y}-\hat{L}_{y}\hat{L}_{x}
= \iu \hbar \hat{L}_{z}
\]
This means we cannot simultaneously know all three components exactly.  What is possible is to simultaneously know one component and the total angular momentum operator:
\[
\hat{L}^{2} = \hat{L}_{x}^{2}+\hat{L}_{y}^{2}+\hat{L}_{z}^{2}
\]
$\hat{L}^{2}$ has eigenvalues $\ell(\ell+1)\hbar^{2}$:
\[
\hat{L}^{2}\,|\ell,m_{\ell}\rangle = \ell(\ell+1)\,\hbar^{2}\,|\ell,m_{\ell}\rangle
\]
Projection quantization:
\[
\hat{L}_{z}\,|\ell,m_{\ell}\rangle = m_{\ell}\,\hbar\,|\ell,m_{\ell}\rangle
\]
with $m_{\ell}=-\ell,-\ell+1,\dots,\ell-1,\ell$.

Even when orbital $\ell=0$, particles can still have angular momentum (spin) $\vect{S}$.  It also satisfies $\hat{S}^{2} = \hat{S}_{x}^{2} + \hat{S}_{y}^{2} + \hat{S}_{z}^{2}$ and
\[
\hat{S}^{2}\,|s,m_{s}\rangle = s(s+1)\,\hbar^{2}\,|s,m_{s}\rangle,
\quad
\hat{S}_{z}\,|s,m_{s}\rangle = m_{s}\,\hbar\,|s,m_{s}\rangle
\]
For the electron, for example, $m_{s} = \pm\tfrac{1}{2}$ so $\hat{S}_{z}$ has eigenvalues $\pm\,\hbar/2$.

Magnetic moment in quantum mechanics:
\[
\vect{\mu} = \vect{\mu}_{L} + \vect{\mu}_{S}
\]
where
$\vect{\mu}_{L} = \tfrac{q}{2m}\,\vect{L}$
and
$\vect{\mu}_{S} = g\,\tfrac{q}{2m}\,\vect{S}$
(for the electron: $g \approx 2$).

For silver, the valence electron is in an $s$-orbital ($\ell=0$), so $\vect{\mu}_{L}\approx\vect{0}$.  The remaining contribution is spin: $s=\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $m_{s}=\pm\tfrac{1}{2}$, hence $\mu_{z}$ can take only two values.  This is the reason for the two peaks.

\subsubsection{Repeated measurements}

If we split along $z$, take the $z_{+}$ beam, and split again along $z$, all atoms are deflected into the positive $z$ direction.  In this sense the $Z$-spin looks classical; it is \emph{stable} with respect to repeated measurements.

a physical rotation by $\theta$ acts on the state with half the angle in the two-dimensional Hilbert space.

The essential property of waves is that they can be linearly combined.  
Quantum mechanical states have the same property: 
they are elements in a vector space and we can derive probability distributions from them by squaring.

\subsection{Quantum Key Distribution}\label{sec:quantum-key-distribution}

If a quantum system is measured twice in the same basis, the same result is obtained with certainty. 
However, an intermediate measurement in a different basis disturbs the state and can change the outcome.
So the interactions of a system with its environment become traceable. 
This can be used to detect an eavesdropper in a cryptographic key agreement protocol.

In 1984, Gilles Brassard and Charles Bennett developed the first application of quantum mechanics for cryptographic purposes with such a key agreement protocol (\hl[2]{BB84}).

Assume Alice and Bob want to agree on a secret key; e.g. for \emph{symmetric encryption} (same key for encryption and decryption).
They can proceed as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Alice repeatedly measures a random bit in a random basis (e.g., $Z$ or $X$) and sends the quantum system to Bob.
  \item Bob measures the received system in a randomly chosen basis ($Z$ or $X$).
\end{enumerate}
  For the bits where they used the same basis, the measurements must be the same, unless there has been an eavesdropper, Eve, measuring the system in a different basis during its transmission from Alice to Bob. 
  Since Eve does not know the true basis, the best thing to do for her is to guess it, so her probability of guessing right is 50\%.
  If, she guesses wrong, the bit Bob recieves will be incorrect with a chance of 50\%, so in total, 25\% of the bits will be wrong.
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Alice and Bob communicate over an \emph{public authenticated classical channel} which basis they used, without sharing the measurement results.
  \item They randomly select some of the shared bits, reveal them publicly and check if they match.
  \item If the error rate for those selected bits is high (around 25\%), Eve is present, and they discard the key.
  \item If the error rate is very low: No eavesdropping detected
  \item They keep the remaining unrevealed bits as the secret key.
\end{enumerate}

A symmetric 50/50 beam splitter acts on the state of the photon with
\[
\ket{x} \mapsto \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{x} + \iu \ket{y}\right)
\qquad
\ket{y} \mapsto \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{y} + \iu \ket{x}\right)
\]
where the $\iu$ accounts for the 90° phase shift ($\frac{\pi}{2}$) a reflected photon picks up compare to the transmitted one.
The effect of the mirrors is
\[
\ket{x} \mapsto \eu^{\iu \phi_m} \ket{y} 
\qquad
\ket{y} \mapsto \eu^{\iu \phi_m} \ket{x} 
\]
where $\phi_m$ is the phase shift the photon picks up at the mirror (e.g. $0$, $\frac{\pi}{2}$, $\pi$, depending on the physical implementation).


So as the photon moves through the device, its state evolves as follows
\[
\ket{x} 
\xmapsto{\text{BS1}}
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{x} + \iu \ket{y}\right)
\xmapsto{\text{Mirrors}}
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\eu^{\iu \phi_m} \ket{y} + \iu \eu^{\iu \phi_m} \ket{x}\right)
\xmapsto{\text{BS2}}
\eu^{\iu \left(\phi_m + \frac{\pi}{2}\right)} \ket{x}
\]
and therefore it will be measured at Detector 1 with certainty.

If, however, one \emph{measures} wether tha photon has gone through the upper or lower arm of the interferometer,
then the state before the second beam splitter is either $\ket{x}$ or $\ket{y}$.
In the second beam splitter the state of the photon is then mapped to either
$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{x} + \iu \ket{y}\right)$
or
$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{y} + \iu \ket{x}\right)$.
In both cases, the photon is detected in either of the detectors with equal probability.



\subsection{Quantum Bit}

To transfer a bit $b \in \{0, 1\}$ into the quantum world, we associate $0$ and $1$ with two orthogonal vectors, usually with the standard basis vectors,
\[
\ket{0} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
\qquad
\ket{1} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}
\]
A general quantum state can then be written as a superpositions
\[
\ket{\psi} = \alpha \ket{0} + \beta \ket{1}
\]
with $\alpha, \beta \in \C$ and $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$.
Measuring $\ket{\psi}$ in the standard basis yields $0$ with probability $|\alpha|^2$ and $1$ with probability $|\beta|^2$.

Quantum circuits are composed of quantum gates, i.e, \emph{unitary maps}.

An important one is the \hl{Hadamard gate},
\[
\matr{H} 
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
1 & -1
\end{bmatrix}
\]
which maps the basis states to superpositions 
\[
\matr{H} \ket{0} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{0} + \ket{1}\right)
\qquad
\matr{H} \ket{1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{0} - \ket{1}\right)
\]
and when applied twice, yields the basis states again, i.e.,
$\matr{H}^2 \ket{0} = \ket{0}$
and
$\matr{H}^2 \ket{1} = \ket{1}$.

Another interesting gate is 
\[
\matr{F} 
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \iu}}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & \iu \\
\iu & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\]
because applying it twice yields the NOT-gate,
\[
\matr{F}^2 
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & 1 \\
1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
\]
which is why it is called ``square root of NOT'', $\matr{F} = \sqrt{\text{NOT}}$.
There is no classical gate that yields the NOT-gate in this way.

\subsection{The Aspect/Gisin/Zeilinger Experiments}\label{sec:aspect-gisin-zeilinger-experiments}

A pair of Qbits (e.g. polarized photons) can exhibit correlations that cannot be explained as merely two independent systems plus shared classical randomness.  
This happens when the two Qbits are prepared in an \emph{entangled} state.

A first example is
\(
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{0}\otimes\ket{0}+\ket{1}\otimes\ket{1}\right)
\).
If Alice and Bob both measure in the standard basis, they always obtain the same bit.  
This looks similar to a classical correlation, but quantum theory says that the individual outcomes are still random and only become fixed upon measurement.

\begin{claim}[Einstein, Podolsky, Rosen, 1935]\label{claim:epr}
Quantum theory is incomplete and must be augmented by ``hidden parameters'' completely determining the measurement outcomes of all alternative measurements.
\end{claim}

Almost 30 years later, Bell came up with the idea that \autoref{claim:epr} can be tested by allowing Alice and Bob to choose between different measurement bases.

\begin{claim}[Bell, 1964]\label{claim:bell}
\autoref{claim:epr} is in doubt:
There exist quantum correlations that go beyond the explanatory power of shared classical information.
\end{claim}

For that purpose, consider the \emph{singlet} state
\[
\ket{\Psi^{-}}
\coloneq
\frac{\ket{0}\otimes\ket{1}-\ket{1}\otimes\ket{0}}{\sqrt{2}}
\]

We rewrite the singlet in rotated measurement bases of Alice and Bob.
\[
\begin{bmatrix}
\ket{0}\\[0.2em]
\ket{1}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\alpha & -\sin\alpha\\
\sin\alpha & \cos\alpha
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\ket{0'}\\[0.2em]
\ket{1'}
\end{bmatrix}
\]

Let Alice rotate her basis by an angle $\alpha_A$ and Bob by $\alpha_B$.
% \[
% \begin{aligned}
% \ket{0}_A &= \cos(\alpha_A)\ket{0_A'}-\sin(\alpha_A)\ket{1_A'} \\
% \ket{1}_A &= \sin(\alpha_A)\ket{0_A'}+\cos(\alpha_A)\ket{1_A'} \\
% \\
% \ket{0}_B &= \cos(\alpha_B)\ket{0_B'}-\sin(\alpha_B)\ket{1_B'} \\
% \ket{1}_B &= \sin(\alpha_B)\ket{0_B'}+\cos(\alpha_B)\ket{1_B'}
% \end{aligned}
% \]
Substituting this into the singlet state yields
\[
\begin{aligned}
\ket{\Psi^-}
&=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\Bigl(
\ket{0}_A\otimes\ket{1}_B
-
\ket{1}_A\otimes\ket{0}_B
\Bigr)
\\
&=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\Bigl(
(\cos\alpha_A\ket{0_A'}-\sin\alpha_A\ket{1_A'})
\otimes
(\sin\alpha_B\ket{0_B'}+\cos\alpha_B\ket{1_B'})
\\
&\hspace{1.6cm}
-
(\sin\alpha_A\ket{0_A'}+\cos\alpha_A\ket{1_A'})
\otimes
(\cos\alpha_B\ket{0_B'}-\sin\alpha_B\ket{1_B'})
\Bigr)\\
&=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\Bigl(
\sin(\alpha_A-\alpha_B)\ket{0_A'}\otimes\ket{0_B'}
+
\cos(\alpha_A-\alpha_B)\ket{0_A'}\otimes\ket{1_B'}
\\
&\hspace{1.6cm}
-
\cos(\alpha_A-\alpha_B)\ket{1_A'}\otimes\ket{0_B'}
+
\sin(\alpha_A-\alpha_B)\ket{1_A'}\otimes\ket{1_B'}
\Bigr)
\end{aligned}
\]



Thus the probabilities depend only on the difference of the angles,
\(
\Delta \coloneq \alpha_A-\alpha_B
\):
\[
\begin{aligned}
\Prob(\text{same})
&=
\Prob(0_A'0_B')+\Prob(1_A'1_B')
=
\sin^2(\Delta)
\\
\Prob(\text{opposite})
&=
\Prob(0_A'1_B')+\Prob(1_A'0_B')
=
\cos^2(\Delta)
\end{aligned}
\]

% Hence if the measurement bases differ by an angle $\Delta$ then
% \(
% \Prob(\text{opposite})=\cos^2(\Delta)
% \).
In particular, if both parties measure in the same basis
\(
\Delta=0
\)
then
\(
\Prob(\text{opposite})=1
\),
so their outcomes are perfectly anti-correlated.

Assume now, in the spirit of \autoref{claim:epr}, that the outcomes of all possible measurements are already fixed in the preparation stage.

Then Alice has two potential output bits $A_1,A_2$ and Bob analogously $B_1,B_2$.

In any single run each party reveals only one of these bits, but the hidden-variable assumption says that all four bits already have predetermined values.

Under the hidden-variable assumption, the four bits $A_1,A_2,B_1,B_2$ exist simultaneously.  
If $A_2\neq B_2$, then at least one of the equalities
$A_1=B_1$, $A_1=B_2$, or $A_2=B_1$
must fail.
Hence we obtain (the simplest example of) a so-called Bell inequality
\[
\Prob(A_2 \neq B_2)
\leq
\Prob(A_1 \neq B_1) + \Prob(A_1 \neq B_2) + \Prob(A_2 \neq B_1)
=
0 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{4}
=
\frac{1}{2}
\]
by the transitivity of equality and the union bound.

But quantum theory predicts
\[
\Prob(A_2=B_2)=\cos^2(60^\circ)=\frac14
\]
and therefore
\[
\Prob(A_2\neq B_2)=\frac34
\]
which violates the Bell inequality.

Thus the observed correlations cannot be explained by pre-shared classical information.

Quantum correlations are stronger than any correlations obtainable from local hidden variables.

This phenomenon is called \emph{Bell non-locality}.
It shows that entanglement produces correlations that arise only upon measurement and cannot be understood as predetermined data carried by the particles.

This challenges the ``Reichenbach principle'':
\begin{quote}
Any correlation between two observed events must arise either from a common cause in their shared past, or from a direct causal influence between the events.
\end{quote}

Bell's result rules out the first explanation.
The correlations cannot be explained by shared classical information created in the common past.

The second explanation would contradict the spirit of relativity theory, since the speed of that influence would have to be highly superluminal.

Experiments have repeatedly confirmed the predictions of quantum theory.
In some experiments the measurements are spacelike separated, meaning that in each observer's reference frame their own measurement occurs first.

Quantum physics describes fundamental particles and their interactions, which ultimately involve the exchange and transformation of energy.
Even mass itself is a form of energy.
However, energy alone does not fully describe a physical system.
We also need information: a specification of the system's possible states.
Thus, physical reality is characterized by the interplay between energy and information.

To understand \autoref{law:second-law-thermodynamics-entropy}, we need a precise notion of entropy.
It is often described as a ``measure of disorder'', but this interpretation is subjective and can be misleading.
A more precise viewpoint is that entropy quantifies the \hl[2]{amount of information required to describe a system}.

In the $19^{\text{th}}$ century, Ludwig Boltzmann connected thermodynamics with the microscopic behavior of particles.
He showed that entropy is related to the number of possible microscopic configurations (microstates) corresponding to a macroscopic state (macrostate).
If \(\Omega\) denotes this number, then
\[
\begin{boxed}
{
S = \kB \ln(\Omega)
}
\end{boxed}
\]
which is the famous equation that is engraved on his tombstone.
Thus, systems with many possible configurations have high entropy, since specifying their exact state requires more information.

In a closed system, the total energy is constant.
\end{law}

In a closed system, the total entropy (``disorder'') does not decrease.
\end{law}
A remarkable feature of \autoref{law:second-law-thermodynamics-entropy} is that it is \emph{asymmetric} in time, whereas, e.g., the laws of classical mechanics are time-symmetric.

\begin{definition}[Entropy]\label{def:entropy}
The entropy (or uncertainty) of a random variable $X$ over $\mathcal{X}$ with PMF $p_X(\cdot)$ is
\[
\begin{verticalhack}
\boxed{
H(X)
\coloneq
\Exp[-\log_2 p_X(X)]
=
- \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p_X(x) \log_2 p_X(x)
}
\end{verticalhack}
\qedhere
\]
\end{definition}
\nameref{def:entropy} describes the uncertainty of a random experiment with given PMF.

An important observation is that the entropy of $X$ does not depend on the actual values of $X$, but only on the probabilities $p_X(x)$.
Hence, \autoref{def:entropy} can easily be extended to random vectors, by just replacing $X$ with $\vect{X}$ and $x$ with $\vect{x}$ in the equation.

\begin{definition}[Mutual information]\label{def:mutual-information}
The mutual information between two random variables $X$, $Y$ is
\[
I(X;Y)
\coloneq
H(X) - H(X \mid Y)
=
H(Y) - H(Y \mid X)
\qedhere
\]
\end{definition}

Another nice form for the mutual information is
\[
I(X;Y) 
=
H(X) + H(Y) - H(X, Y)
\]
because it shows its symmetry.

\begin{definition}[Kullback-Leibler divergence]\label{def:kullback-leibler-divergence}
Let $P(\cdot)$ and $Q(\cdot)$ be two PMFs over the same finite or countable alphabet $\mathcal{X}$.
The \emph{relative entropy} or \emph{Kullback-Leibler divergence} between $P$ and $Q$ is
\[
\begin{verticalhack}
% D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q)
\kld{P}{Q}
\coloneq
\Exp_{X \sim P}\left[\log_2 \frac{P(X)}{Q(X)}\right]
=
\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} P(x) \log_2 \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}
\end{verticalhack}
\qedhere
\]
\end{definition}

Unlike \nameref{def:mutual-information}, \nameref{def:kullback-leibler-divergence} is not symmetric.
It should not be thought of as a distance, but rather as an ``energy'' (it actually describes the inefficiency of assuming that the PMF is $Q(\cdot)$ when the true distribution is $P(\cdot)$.).

\nameref{def:mutual-information} and \nameref{def:kullback-leibler-divergence} are related to one another via
\[
I(X;Y)
=
\kld{p_{X,Y}}{p_X \cdot p_Y}
\]

\nameref{def:mutual-information} can therefore be interpreted as deviance of the actual joint distribution from if they were treated independently (while keeping the same marginals).

\begin{example}\label{ex:entropy-uniform-distribution}
In case of a uniform distribution, i.e. $p_X(x) = 1/|\mathcal{X}|$ for all $x \in \mathcal{X}$, the entropy of $X$ is the logarithm of the size of its range, i.e., $H(X) = \log_2 |\mathcal{X}|$.
\end{example}

If all microstates in a macrostates ``look roughly the same'' (this condition is the translation of the uniformity condition from \autoref{ex:entropy-uniform-distribution}), we have
\[
H(\text{macrostate})
\approx
\log_2(\text{\# corresponding microstates})
\]

The ``physical entropy'', as opposed to the information-theoretic one, is usually denoted by $S$ and has an additional factor of 
$\kB \ln(2)$,
where $\kB \approx 1.38 \cdot 10^{-23} \text{Joule}/\text{Kelvin}$ is the Boltzmann constant
and
the $\ln(2)$ is a common view at the border between nature and abstraction since $2$ is a ``logical'' constant and $\eu$ is a ``natural'' constant.
If we denote $\Omega \coloneq \text{\# microstates}$, we obtain the famous formula
\[
S
=
\kB 
\ln(2) 
H
=
\kB \ln(\Omega)
\]
derived by Boltzmann in the 1870s and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann%27s_entropy_formula}{engraved on his tombstone}.



\begin{example}[$1$-Molecule Gas]\label{ex:one-molecule-gas}
Consider a ``one-molecule gas'' in a container of volume $V$.
The first macrostate corresponds to the particle being anywhere in the container, where for the second, smaller macrostate, the particle is confined to the left half of the container.

\underline{Remark:}
% \begin{itemize}
% \item
% Obviously, not all microstates in a macrostate look the ame; forn instance it is probably much more likely that the particle is close to the middle of the container than to one of its corners.
% \item
We cannot simply \emph{count} the number of possible positions and momenta of the particle, since it is (uncountably) infinite for both macrostates.
Indeed, there also exist definitions of entropy for the continuous case, but we do not need that here as long as we are only interested in entropy \emph{differences}:
Assume that we distinguish different microstates only up to some fixed finite precision, i.e., we put a grid of certain ``density'' (e.g. cubes with side length $\delta$) into the respective volumes and the particles position is described by the coordinates of the cube it is in.
% \end{itemize}

Then, no matter the actual value of $\delta$, there are exactly twice as many microstates corresponding to the first macrostate than to the second.
If we denote the number of microstates corresponding to the first macrostate by $\Omega_1$ and the number of microstates corresponding to the second macrostate by $\Omega_2 = \Omega_1 / 2$, we have, the entropy difference thus is
\[
% \begin{aligned}
\Delta H 
=
H(\text{macrostate 2}) - H(\text{macrostate 1}) 
=
\log_2(\Omega_2) - \log_2(\Omega_1) 
=
\log_2\left(\frac{\Omega_2}{\Omega_1}\right) 
% =
% \log_2\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)
=
-1
% \end{aligned}
\]
or, in terms of physical entropy, $\Delta S_{\text{gas}}
 = \kB \ln(2) \Delta H = -\kB \ln(2)$.
\end{example}

Consider a gas of $N$ molecules in a container of volume $V$.
We regard the gas as the \emph{system} and the surroundings as a heat reservoir (the \emph{environment}) at temperature $T$.

Compare the following two macrostates:
\begin{itemize}
  \item macrostate $1$: all molecules may be anywhere in the container (volume $V$)
  \item macrostate $2$: all molecules are confined to the left half of the container (volume $V/2$)
\end{itemize}

As in the one-molecule case, we discretize position space into cells of volume $\delta^3$.
Then the number of position microstates for a single molecule is proportional to the accessible volume.
Since the momentum space is the same in both macrostates, it cancels in the ratio, so it suffices to consider position space.

Assuming the molecules are non-interacting so that their positions are independent, the total number of microstates is
\(
\Omega_1 \propto V^N
\),
\(
\Omega_2 \propto \left({V}/{2}\right)^N
\).
% Hence
% \[
% \frac{\Omega_2}{\Omega_1}
% =
% \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^N
% \]
% Since each molecule independently has probability $\frac{1}{2}$ of being in the left half of the container, the probability that all $N$ molecules are in the left half is also $2^{-N}$.
Therefore, the entropy difference is
\[
% \begin{aligned}
\Delta H
=
\log_2(\Omega_2) - \log_2(\Omega_1)
=
\log_2\!\left(\frac{\Omega_2}{\Omega_1}\right)
=
\log_2\!\left(2^{-N}\right)
=
- N
% \end{aligned}
\]
or, in physical units,
\(
\Delta S_{\text{gas}}
=
\kB \ln(2)\, \Delta H
=
- N \kB \ln(2)
\).
This transition (compressing all mole\-cules into half the volume) is highly improbable, with probability $2^{-N}$, but it can be enforced by investing work, e.g., by using a piston to compress the gas.
We now interpret this entropy decrease thermodynamically and relate it to the energy required to enforce the compression.
The gas is then not an isolated system: work is performed on it and, after thermalization, energy is transferred as heat to the environment, increasing the entropy around the gas container,
so there is no contradiction to \autoref{law:second-law-thermodynamics-entropy}.

A certain amount of work $\Delta W$ is invested to compress the gas, which, after thermalization, is dissipated as heat $\Delta Q$ to the environment (\autoref{law:first-law-thermodynamics-energy}).
It is that heat transfer that is responsible for the increase of entropy in the environment, which is necessary to compensate for the decrease of entropy in the gas and thus to avoid a violation of \autoref{law:second-law-thermodynamics-entropy}.

% A heat reservoir of temperature $T$ that receives an amount $\Delta Q$ of heat energy increases its entropy by at least $\frac{\Delta Q}{T}$, with equality in the reversible case (Clausius).
% For a thermodynamic system at temperature $T$, Clausius gives
% For the environment (an ideal heat reservoir), Clausius gives
% \[
% \Delta S_{\text{env}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{T}
% \]
% where $T$ is the temperature of the heat reservoir.
% A heat reservoir is defined to be ideal in the sense that it is completely reversible, i.e. the heat transfer occurs without any additional entropy production (i.e. excluding processes such as heat flow across a finite temperature difference, friction, diffusion, chemical reactions, or other irreversible processes).

For the environment, modeled as a heat reservoir at temperature $T$, the entropy change is
\[
\Delta S_{\text{env}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{T}
\]
where $\Delta Q>0$ denotes the heat transferred to the environment.
A heat reservoir is an idealized system that remains in internal equilibrium at fixed temperature $T$ while exchanging heat with other systems. 
% This does not imply that the overall process is reversible: 
% additional entropy production may still occur elsewhere, for example due to heat flow across a finite temperature difference, friction, diffusion, or other irreversible effects.

\autoref{law:second-law-thermodynamics-entropy} requires
\[
\Delta S_{\text{tot}} = \Delta S_{\text{gas}} + \Delta S_{\text{env}} \geq 0
\]
where equality holds only in the reversible case, i.e. without any additional entropy production (i.e. excluding irreversible processes such as heat flow across a finite temperature difference, friction, diffusion, turbulence, chemical reactions, ...).
Thus, the minimal required work to enforce this entropy decrease is obtained in the reversible case:
\[
\Delta W
=
\Delta Q 
=
- T 
\Delta S_{\text{gas}} 
=
N \kB T \ln(2)
\]

We obtain the same result by using the ideal gas law to compute the work needed to compress the gas from volume \(V\) to volume \(V/2\): 
\[
p V = N \kB T 
\quad
\Longleftrightarrow
\quad
p(V) = \frac{N \kB T}{V}
\]
and thus
\[
\begin{verticalhack}
- \int_{V}^{V/2} p(V) \dif V
=
- \int_{V}^{V/2} \frac{N \kB T}{V} \dif V
=
N \kB T \ln(2)
\end{verticalhack}
\qedhere
\]
\end{example}


We can interpret the molecule in \autoref{ex:one-molecule-gas} as storing one bit of information, $0$ if it is in the left halft and $1$ if it is in the right half of the container.
If we force the molecule to be in the left half, it corresponds to erasing that bit of information, in the sense that its state is $0$ at the end, regardless of its initial state.
% The price of this erasure is $\kB T \ln(2)$ of work.
\begin{claim}[Landauer's principle]\label{claim:landauer-principle}
Erasing one bit - irrelevant by what physical system it is stored - requires at least
\[
\kB T \ln(2) \quad(\approx 3 \cdot 10^{-21} \text{Joule} \quad \text{at room temperature})
\]
of free energy, which, in the process, must be dissipated as heat to the environment.
\end{claim}

\subsection{Converse of \nameref{claim:landauer-principle}}

The inverse process of erasure, let's call it \emph{randomization}, allows for gaining free energy.
\begin{example}
The work value of the all-$0$-string of length $N$, however it is represented physically, is $N \kB T \ln(2)$.
This amount of environmental heat energy can be transformed into work.
\end{example}


The Bennett work value of a general string $s$ of length $N$ is approximately
\[
W \approx (N - K(s)) \, \kB T \ln(2)
\]
where $K(s)$ denotes the Kolmogorov complexity of the string $s$, i.e., the length of the shortest program that outputs $s$.



\autoref{claim:landauer-principle} and its converse can be extended to $b$-ary digits:
If, instead of binary digits, we work with $b$-ary digits, the work value of the all-$0$-string of length $N$ is $N \kB T \ln(b)$.

\begin{example}\label{ex:pi-work-value}
The string $\pi_N$ admits a short description: there exists a fixed program
that computes the digits of $\pi$, together with an encoding of the number $N$
specifying how many digits to output. Therefore,
\[
K(\pi_N) = O(\log N)
\]
since encoding the integer $N$ requires about $\log N$ bits.

Therefore the Bennett work value of the first $N$ decimal digits of $\pi$ is approximately
\[
W \approx \bigl(N \ln(10) - O(\log N)\bigr)\,\kB T
\]
which, for large $N$, is essentially $N\kB T \ln(10)$, i.e.,maximal.
\end{example}

\autoref{ex:pi-work-value} shows that the work value is related to \emph{reversible data compression}: 
Exaclty those strings have work value that can be compressed to a shorter length in a lossless fashion.
\begin{example}
A string of length $N$ resulting from flips of an unfair coin with probabilities $p$ and $(1-p)$ has, most likely, work value 
$(1 - h(p)) N \kB T \ln(2)$, where 
\[
h(p) \coloneq -p \log_2 p - (1-p) \log_2 (1-p)
\]
is the binary entropy function.
\end{example}



\begin{example}
$r$ copies of the same, perhaps incompressible string of length $N$ have work value at least $(r - 1) N \kB T \ln(2)$.
\end{example}


\subsection{Benett's Solution to Maxwell's Demon}

In the face of \nameref{claim:landauer-principle}, the paradox disappears:
The demon must erase the information it gathers during the sorting process. 
This erasure requires a minimal amount of work and leads to heat dissipation into the environment, which compensates exactly for the entropy decrease created in the gas.

The demon must maintain an internal memory storing measurement outcomes. 
Even a one-bit memory must be reset after each step, and overwriting information necessarily involves erasure. 
Thus, forgetting information has a thermodynamic cost.
If the demon has a large memory, it can temporarily store many observations (effectively recording the initial disorder of the gas).
However, to reuse its memory, it must eventually erase this information, and this erasure restores the total entropy balance.

Conversely, possessing information (or redundancy) allows one to extract work: 
knowing the state of a system (e.g. in \autoref{ex:one-molecule-gas}, if the molecule is in the left half or the right half of the container) can be used to convert environmental heat energy into free mechanical energy. 
In this sense, information has an energetic value (``knowledge is energy'').

Nevertheless, no perpetuum mobile of the second kind arises. 
Any work gained through such knowledge must ultimately be paid for when the demon resets its memory, ensuring consistency with \autoref{law:second-law-thermodynamics-entropy}.

\subsection{Reversible Computing}

% Can computation be made locially reversible?
If, in the course of a computation, information is lost about ``which branch the computation came from'', then free energy $\kB T \ln(2)$ must be invested, which is dissipates as heat to the evironment.
In other words, the \emph{logical} irreversibility of a computation (``information is lost'') implies its \emph{thermodynamic} irreversibility (``free energy is lost'').
Thus, it appears advantageous if a computaiton does not have such collisions of branches.

As it turns out, it is (at least in principle) possible to carry out any logically reversible computation in a thermodynamically reversible way: \nameref{claim:landauer-principle} is {``tight''} in this sense.

Let \(N(\omega)\) denote the number of EM modes with frequency at most \(\omega\).
Then the number of modes in the interval \([\omega,\omega+\dif\omega]\) is \(\dif N\).
% The number of modes with frequency in a small interval \([\omega,\omega+\dif\omega]\) is determined by the number of lattice points \(\vect{n}\) in a thin spherical shell between radii \(|\vect{n}|\) and \(|\vect{n}|+\dif |\vect{n}|\).
To estimate \(\dif N\), we count the lattice points \(\vect{n}\) in a thin spherical shell between radii \(|\vect{n}|\) and \(|\vect{n}|+\dif|\vect{n}|\).
For large \(|\vect{n}|\), we approximate this by the volume of that shell in \(\vect{n}\)-space.
Since only the first octant is allowed, the shell volume is
\[
\frac18 \cdot 4\pi |\vect{n}|^2 \,\dif |\vect{n}|
=
\frac{\pi}{2} |\vect{n}|^2 \,\dif |\vect{n}|
\]
Moreover, each wave vector corresponds to two independent polarization states (the two transverse polarizations of the EM field),  % previously we said its about magnetic and electric???
so the number of EM modes is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mode-counting}
\dif N % what is N?
=
2 \cdot \frac{\pi}{2} |\vect{n}|^2 \,\dif |\vect{n}|
=
\pi |\vect{n}|^2 \,\dif |\vect{n}|
\end{equation}
From \eqref{eq:mode-frequency}, we have
\(
|\vect{n}| = \frac{l}{\pi c}\omega
\) and
\(
\dif |\vect{n}| = \frac{l}{\pi c}\,\dif\omega
\).
Substituting these into \eqref{eq:mode-counting}, we obtain
\[
\dif N
=
\pi
\left(\frac{l}{\pi c}\omega\right)^2
\left(\frac{l}{\pi c}\,\dif\omega\right)
=
\frac{l^3}{\pi^2 c^3}\omega^2 \,\dif\omega
\]
Dividing by the cavity volume \(l^3\), we obtain the number of modes per unit volume in the frequency interval \([\omega,\omega+\dif\omega]\),
\[
\frac{\dif N}{l^3}
=
g(\omega)\,\dif\omega
=
\frac{\omega^2}{\pi^2 c^3}\,\dif\omega
\]
where
\(
g(\omega)=\frac{\omega^2}{\pi^2 c^3}
\)
is the density of modes per unit volume per unit angular frequency.


% Thus the \emph{density of modes} grows quadratically with the frequency.

\subsubsection{Classical equipartition and the Rayleigh-Jeans law}

In classical statistical mechanics, the equipartition theorem says that every quadratic degree of freedom contributes an average energy of \(\frac12 \kB T\).
Each EM mode contributes two such quadratic terms (one electric and one magnetic), so the average energy per mode is of order
\(
\kB T
\).
This can also be seen directly from the Boltzmann distribution
\[
\Prob(E) \propto \eu^{-E/(\kB T)}
\]
Classically, each EM mode behaves like a harmonic oscillator with continuous energy
\(
E \in [0,\infty)
\),
and one obtains
\[
\Exp[E]
=
\frac{\int_0^\infty E\,\eu^{-E/(\kB T)} \dif E}{\int_0^\infty \eu^{-E/(\kB T)} \dif E}
=
\kB T
\]
which is \hl[4]{independent of the frequency \(\omega\)}.
Thus even very high-frequency modes carry the same average energy \(\kB T\).
% So the Boltzmann factor does \emph{not} suppress high-frequency modes in the classical theory; every mode still carries average energy \(\kB T\).
% Therefore, the energy contained in the frequency interval \([\omega,\omega+\dif\omega]\) is proportional to
% \[
% (\text{number of modes in $[\omega,\omega+\dif\omega]$}) \cdot \kB T
% \]
% and hence the spectral energy density satisfies
% \(
% u(\omega)\,\dif\omega \propto \omega^2 \kB T \,\dif\omega
% \).

Therefore, the spectral energy density is obtained by multiplying the mode density by the average energy per mode:
% \[
% u(\omega)\,\dif\omega
% =
% g(\omega)\,\dif\omega
% \cdot 
% \kB T
% \]
% % Hence
% Dividing by \(\dif\omega\), we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rayleigh-jeans-law}
% \boxed{
  u(\omega)
  =
  g(\omega) \cdot \kB T
%   =
%   g(\omega) \cdot
% \kB T
=
  \frac{\kB T}{\pi^2 c^3}\,\omega^2
% }
\end{equation}
which is the \hl[2]{Rayleigh-Jeans law}.
Thus the energy density grows quadratically without bound as the frequency increases.

\subsubsection{Ultraviolet catastrophe}

The total energy density is obtained by integrating over all frequencies,
\[
\int_0^\infty u(\omega)\,\dif\omega
\overset{\text{\eqref{eq:rayleigh-jeans-law}}}{=}
\frac{\kB T}{\pi^2 c^3}\int_0^\infty \omega^2\,\dif\omega
=
\infty
\]
Thus classical theory predicts an infinite amount of energy stored in the EM field inside the cavity.

This is the \hl[2]{ultraviolet catastrophe}.
The term refers to the absurd prediction that most of the energy should be concentrated at very high frequencies (ultraviolet, X-ray, and beyond).
If that were true, ordinary objects at room temperature would emit enormous amounts of high-energy radiation, which is not observed.

Experimentally, the Rayleigh-Jeans law (fortunately for us) is only correct for low frequencies.
For high frequencies, the spectral energy density does \emph{not} continue to grow quadratically; instead, it decreases rapidly.




\subsubsection{Quantization}

Max Planck resolved this problem by abandoning the classical assumption that energy can be exchanged continuously.
He postulated that a mode of frequency \(\omega\) can absorb or emit energy only in discrete packets,
\[
E_n = n \hbar \omega
\]
with \(n \in \N_0\) and
\(
\hbar 
=
\frac{h}{2\pi}
\approx 1.054 \cdot 10^{-34}\,\mathrm{J\,s}
\)
(reduced Planck constant).
For black-body radiation, it is sufficient to consider the thermal excitation energies \(n\hbar\omega\), i.e. we ignore the zero-point offset \(\frac12\hbar\omega\), since it does not affect the frequency-dependent thermal spectrum.

The Boltzmann factor is still the same as in the classical case,
\[
\Prob(E) \propto \eu^{-E/(\kB T)}
\]
but now the allowed energies are no longer continuous.
Instead of all \(E \in [0,\infty)\), only the discrete values
\(
0,\hbar\omega,2\hbar\omega,\dots
\)
are possible.

This changes the statistics drastically.
For large \(\omega\), even the first excited state already has the large energy \(\hbar\omega\), 
% so the probability that the mode is excited at all is strongly suppressed by the Boltzmann factor.
so nonzero occupation of the mode is strongly suppressed by the Boltzmann factor.
Thus the average energy per mode decreases for large \(\omega\).

In fact, the average energy of one mode becomes
\[
\Exp[E]
=
\frac{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n\hbar\omega\,\eu^{-n\hbar\omega/(\kB T)}}{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\eu^{-n\hbar\omega/(\kB T)}}
=
\frac{\hbar\omega}{\eu^{\hbar\omega/(\kB T)}-1}
\]
which is \hl[4]{\emph{not} independent of \(\omega\)} anymore!
For small \(\omega\), however, this is still approximately \(\kB T\), so the classical result is recovered.
But for large \(\omega\) it decreases exponentially.

% Multiplying again by the number of modes, which scales like \(\omega^2\), yields the spectral energy density
Multiplying again by the density of modes \(g(\omega)\) yields the spectral energy density
\[
\boxed{
u(\omega)
=
\frac{\hbar \omega^3}{\pi^2 c^3}
\frac{1}{\eu^{\hbar\omega/(\kB T)}-1}
}
\]
which is known as the \emph{Planck law}.%

\subsection{Photoelectric Effect}


In 1887, Heinrich Hertz studied the emission of electrons from a metal surface illuminated by light.

% \subsubsection{Classical expectation}

Classically, light is an EM wave whose energy is carried continuously by the wave amplitude.
Thus one would expect:
Increasing the intensity increases the energy transferred to each electron,
 therefore the kinetic energy of the emitted electrons should increase with intensity.
The color of the light, i.e. its frequency, should play no essential role.
Even low-frequency light should eventually eject electrons if one waits long enough (there should be a \emph{lag time}).



% \subsubsection{Observation}

But, these expectations are not confirmed experimentally, instead the observed behavior is:
Increasing the intensity increases mainly the \emph{number} of emitted electrons.
The kinetic energy of the emitted electrons depends on the frequency of the light.
Below a certain threshold frequency \(\omega_0\), no electrons are emitted at all, regardless of the intensity or duration of illumination.
The emission is essentially immediate once the frequency is above the threshold.

Thus the crucial quantity is not the intensity, but the frequency.

% \subsubsection{Einstein's explanation}

In 1905, Einstein explained this by assuming that light is absorbed in discrete quanta, later called \emph{photons}.
A photon of angular frequency \(\omega\) carries the energy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:photon-energy}
\boxed{
E_{\gamma} 
= 
\hbar \omega
=
\frac{h c}{\lambda}
}
\end{equation}
where \(\lambda\) is the wavelength of the light.
To remove an electron from the metal, one must overcome the \emph{work function}
\[
W = \hbar \omega_0
\]
where \(\omega_0\) is the threshold frequency.
Hence emission is possible only if
\(
\hbar \omega \geq W
\), regardless of the intensity of the light.
\[

The kinetic energy of the emitted electron is then
% \begin{equation}\label{eq:photoelectric}
\[
E_{\mathrm{kin}} 
= 
\hbar \omega - W
=
\hbar (\omega - \omega_0)
\]
% \end{equation}
Thus:
\begin{itemize}
  \item increasing the frequency increases the energy of each photon and therefore the kinetic energy of the emitted electrons
  \item increasing the intensity increases only the number of photons and therefore mainly the number of emitted electrons (if the frequency is above the threshold)
\end{itemize}

The photoelectric effect shows that light cannot be understood as a purely classical wave.
Its energy is exchanged in discrete packets.
Together with black-body radiation, this was one of the key pieces of evidence for quantization.

At the same time, light still exhibits interference and polarization phenomena.
Thus quantum theory combines both aspects:
\begin{itemize}
\item waves behave like particles
\item particles behave like waves
\end{itemize}

\begin{interlude}[Hilbert spaces]\label{interlude:hilbert-spaces}
The state spaces of quantum-mechanical systems are generally \emph{Hilbert spaces}, i.e., complete vector space over the complex numbers with an inner product.
\emph{Quantum informatics} happens in \emph{finite}-dimensional Hilbert spaces, but in general, they can be (uncountably) infinite-dimensional.
%
An important result states that, up to isomorphism, Hilbert spaces are classified by the dimension of an orthonormal basis.

A normalized wave packet $\psi$ is an element of an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space, and the inner product is defined as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inner-product}
\braket{\psi_1 | \psi_2} 
\coloneq
\int_{\R^3} \psi_1^*(\vect{x}, t) \psi_2(\vect{x}, t)\,\dif\vect{x}
\end{equation}
For the integral to be well-defined, we have to restrict to square-integrable functions.
In order to allow for normalization, the factor $C$ in \eqref{eq:plane-wave} has to be an envelope that makes the integral finite.
This yields a wave packet that is then again a superposition of plane waves.
\end{interlude}

Now we want to relate the parameters of \eqref{eq:plane-wave} to particle properties:
\begin{itemize}
\item 
Louis de Broglie had the idea to associate the momentum of a particle with its quantum mechanical wave vector as
\[
\vect{p} = \hbar \vect{k}
\]
and, thus:
\(
\vect{k} = \vect{p} / \hbar
\)
\item 
We already introduced the energy in \eqref{eq:photon-energy}.
As in classical, non-relativistic mechanics, we relate it to the momentum as
\[
E = \hbar \omega = \frac{\|\vect{p}\|^2}{2m}
\]
and, thus: 
\(
\omega = \|\vect{p}\|^2 / (2 m \hbar)
\)
\end{itemize}

matter wave of a free particle (no potential, no walls) with mass $m$ and momentum $\vect{p}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:matter-wave}
\boxed{

}
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
If C in \eqref{eq:matter-wave} does not depend on $\vect{x}$ and $t$, then $\psi(\vect{x}, t)$ is a plane wave, i.e. an idealized free-particle state that is spatially extended infinitely in all directions.
That corresponds to a particle that is not localized in space at all, so it kind of everywhere equally.
But when the idea came up that the wave characterizes the probability of finding the particle at a certain position, it was realized that such a plane wave is not a normalizable physical state, since there is no uniform probability distribution over the whole space that is normalized.
Therefore, $C(\vect{x}, t)$ is usually some kind of a Gaussian or other envelope that limits the spatial extent of the wave.
% For example, an electron in a hydrogen atom, would have some kind of a circular distribution.
\end{remark}

&= -\hbar^2 
\cdot 
\left(\tfrac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} + \tfrac{\partial^2}{\partial y^2} + \tfrac{\partial^2}{\partial z^2}\right) \psi(\vect{x}, t) 
\cdot 
\left(\frac{- \iu}{2 m \hbar}\right) \\
&= \hbar \cdot \Delta \psi(\vect{x}, t) \cdot \frac{\iu}{2 m}\tikzmark{laplace}
\begin{tikzpicture}[remember picture, overlay, font=\tiny, green]%
\node [anchor=west, align=left, text width=3.8cm] at ($(pic cs:laplace)+(0.5,1ex/2)$) {$\Delta$ is the Laplace operator};
\end{tikzpicture}%
\end{aligned}
\]
which directly corresponds to the Schrödinger equation for a free particle:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schroedinger-free-particle}
\boxed{
\iu \hbar \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi(\vect{x}, t) = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2 m} \cdot \Delta \psi(\vect{x}, t)
}
\end{equation}

The left hand side of the (non-relativistic) Schrödinger equation always looks like in \eqref{eq:schroedinger-free-particle}.
The right hand side changes if we have a particle that is not free, e.g. in a potential $V(\vect{x})$.



\begin{interlude}[continues=interlude:hilbert-spaces]
The scalar product \eqref{eq:inner-product} defines a norm 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:norm}
  \|\psi\| 
  = 
  \sqrt{\braket{\psi|\psi}}
  =
  \left(\int_{\R^3} |\psi(\vect{x}, t)|^2 \,\dif\vect{x}\right)^{\!\!1/2}
\end{equation}
on the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$.
\end{interlude}

Properties:
\begin{itemize}
  \item linearity: 
  if $\psi_1$ and $\psi_2$ are solutions, then any linear combination $\alpha \psi_1 + \beta \psi_2$ is also a solution.
  \begin{itemize}
    \item not surprising, since we derived it to be linear. this makes quantum mechanics a linear theory.
    \item entanglement (\autoref{sec:aspect-gisin-zeilinger-experiments}) comes out of this: if we have two Qbits, i.e. $\ket{00}$ and $\ket{11}$ are both possible states of the system, then $\alpha \ket{00} + \beta \ket{11}$ is also a possible state, and now we have an entangled state.
  \end{itemize}
  \item preserves inner product:
  A solution can be written as $\psi(\vect{x}, t) = U(t) \psi(\vect{x}, 0)$.
  Define $\psi_0 = \psi(\cdot, 0)$.
  Then the norm square can be written as
  \[
  \|\psi(\cdot, t)\|^2
  =
  \braket{U(t)\psi_0|U(t)\psi_0}
  =
  \braket{\psi_0|U(t)^* U(t)\psi_0}
  \overset{!}{=}
  \braket{\psi_0|\psi_0} 
  = 
  1
  \]
  where the penultimate step holds iff $U(t)$ is unitary, i.e. $U(t)^* U(t) = \id$.
  Thus, the time evolution operator $U(t)$ is required to be unitary.
  This also implies that time evolution in quantum mechanics is reversible with $U(t)^{-1} = U(t)^*$.
  \begin{itemize}
  \item $\int_{\R^3} |\psi(\vect{x}, 0)|^2 \,\dif\vect{x} = 1$ implies $\int_{\R^3} |\psi(\vect{x}, t)|^2 \,\dif\vect{x} = 1$ for all $t \in \R$.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}



\begin{definition}[adjoint operator]
The adjoint $A^*$ of a linear operator $A$ is defined as
\[
\braket{\psi_1|A\psi_2} = \braket{A^*\psi_1|\psi_2}
\]
where $\braket{\cdot|\cdot}$ denotes the inner product defined in \eqref{eq:inner-product}.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}%[Hemitian]
\label{def:hermitian}
a linear operator $A$ is \hl[2]{self-adjoint} or Hermitian iff $A^* = A$
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}%[unitary]
\label{def:unitary}
a linear operator $U$ is \hl[2]{unitary} iff $U U^* = \id$, or, equivalently, $U^* = U^{-1}$
\end{definition}

\subsection{Observables}

% Physical quantities in quantum mechanics are represented by operators acting on the wave function.
Correspondence principle: measurable physical quantities in classical mechanics can be translated to quantum mechanics by using the corresponding self-adjoint (Hermitian) operator, so-called \emph{observable}.
The probability density for finding a particle at position $\vect{x}$ at time $t$ is $|\psi(\vect{x},t)|^2$, and expectation values are computed by integrating this density against the corresponding operator.
We use \eqref{eq:matter-wave} to derive some important observables:

\medskip
\hrule
\textbf{Position}:
\[
% \begin{aligned}
% \Exp_\psi[\vect{x}]
% &= \int_{\R^3} |\psi(\vect{x}, t)|^2 \cdot \vect{x} \,\dif\vect{x} \\
% &= \int_{\R^3} \psi^*(\vect{x}, t) \cdot \vect{x} \cdot \psi(\vect{x}, t) \,\dif\vect{x} \\
% % &= \int_{\R^3} \psi^*(\vect{x}, t) A(\psi)(\vect{x}, t) \,\dif\vect{x} \\
% &= \braket{\psi | \vect{x} \cdot \psi}
% \end{aligned}
\Exp_\psi[\vect{x}]
=
\int_{\R^3} |\psi(\vect{x}, t)|^2 \, \vect{x} \,\dif\vect{x}
=
\int_{\R^3} \psi^*(\vect{x}, t) \, \vect{x} \, \psi(\vect{x}, t) \,\dif\vect{x} 
=
\braket{\psi | \vect{x}  \psi}
\]
operator: 
$
\psi \mapsto \vect{x}  \psi
$
\medskip
\hrule
\textbf{Momentum}:

For the plane wave \eqref{eq:matter-wave},
\(
\nabla \psi(\vect{x}, t)
=
\frac{\iu}{\hbar}\,\vect{p}\,\psi(\vect{x}, t)
\)
and thus
\(
\frac{\hbar}{\iu}\nabla \psi(\vect{x}, t)
=
\vect{p}\,\psi(\vect{x}, t)
\).
This motivates the momentum operator
\[
\psi \mapsto \tfrac{\hbar}{\iu} \nabla \psi
\]
and the expectation value
\[
% \begin{aligned}
\Exp_\psi[\vect{p}]
= \int_{\R^3} \psi^*(\vect{x}, t) \cdot \tfrac{\hbar}{\iu} \nabla \psi(\vect{x}, t) \,\dif\vect{x} 
= \braket{\psi | \tfrac{\hbar}{\iu} \nabla \psi}
% \end{aligned}
\]
% \medskip
\hrule
\textbf{Energy}:

For the plane wave \eqref{eq:matter-wave},
\(
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi(\vect{x}, t)
=
-\frac{\iu E}{\hbar}\,\psi(\vect{x}, t)
\)
and thus
\(
\iu \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi(\vect{x}, t)
=
E\,\psi(\vect{x}, t)
\).
This motivates the energy operator
\[
\psi \mapsto \iu \hbar \tfrac{\partial}{\partial t} \psi
\]
and the expectation value
\[
% \begin{aligned}
\Exp_\psi[E]
= \int_{\R^3} \psi^*(\vect{x}, t) \cdot \iu \hbar \tfrac{\partial}{\partial t} \psi(\vect{x}, t) \,\dif\vect{x} 
= \braket{\psi | \iu \hbar \tfrac{\partial}{\partial t} \psi}
% \end{aligned}
\]
% \medskip
\hrule
\medskip


In classical mechanics, the energy of a free particle is $E = {\|\vect{p}\|^2}/{(2 m)}$.
If we replace the left side with 
$E = \iu \hbar \tfrac{\partial}{\partial t}$ 
and the right side with 
$(\tfrac{\hbar}{\iu} \nabla)^2 / (2 m)$, 
we return to the Schrödinger equation \eqref{eq:schroedinger-free-particle} for a free particle.

The general, non-relativistic, time-dependent Schrödinger equation for a particle in a potential $V(\vect{x}, t)$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schroedinger}
\boxed{
\iu \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi(\vect{x}, t) = \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2 m} \Delta + V(\vect{x}, t)  \right) \psi(\vect{x}, t)
}
\end{equation}
which can be written as
\[
\iu \hbar  \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi(\vect{x}, t) = H  \psi(\vect{x}, t)
\]
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hamiltonian}
H = -\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2 m} \Delta + V(\vect{x}, t)
\end{equation}
is the Hamiltonian operator
% \[
% H 
% % = 
% % T + V
% =
% -\frac{\hbar^2}{2 m} \Delta + V(\vect{x}, t)
% \]
corresponding to the total (kinetic + potential) non-relativistic energy of the system. 
For fixed $t$, $H$ is Hermitian.

For a free particle, 
% $H = -\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2m}\Delta$ since 
$V(\vect{x}, t) = 0$, and \eqref{eq:schroedinger} reduces to \eqref{eq:schroedinger-free-particle}.
In that case, 
$H = -\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2m}\Delta$ 
and 
the general solution is
\[
  \psi(\vect{x}, t) = \eu^{- \iu H t / \hbar} \psi(\vect{x}, 0)
\]
where $\eu^{A} \coloneq \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{A^n}{n!}$ (convergent for some assumptions on the operator $A$).
This yields the time evolution operator $U(t) = \eu^{- \iu H t / \hbar}$.
Since $H$ is Hermitian (\autoref{def:hermitian}), we have
\[
\eu^{- \iu H t / \hbar} \left(\eu^{- \iu H t / \hbar}\right)^*
=
\eu^{- \iu H t / \hbar} \eu^{\iu H^* t / \hbar}
=
\eu^{\iu (H^* - H) t / \hbar}
=
\eu^{0}
=
\id
\]
and hence $U(t)$ is unitary and therefore preserves the norm of the state vector.
In general, if $A$ is Hermitian, then $\eu^{\iu A}$ is unitary.
Furthermore, we have the properties
\begin{itemize}
  \item $U(t_1) \cdot U(t_2) = U(t_1 + t_2)$
  \item $U(0) = \id$
  \item $U(t)^{-1} = U(t)^* = U(-t)$
\end{itemize}











\subsection{Digression on Operators}\label{sec:operators}

\subsubsection{Bounded Operators}

\begin{definition}\label{def:compact-operator}
Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are Banach spaces and $U$ is the open unit ball in $X$.
A linear map $T \colon X \to Y$ is said to be \emph{compact} if the closure of $T(U)$ is compact in $Y$.
It is clear that $T$ is then also bounded.
Thus $T \in B(X,Y)$.
\end{definition}
Equivalently, $T$ is compact if and only if every bounded sequence $(x_n)_n$ in $X$ contains a subsequence $(x_{n_i})_i$ such that $(Tx_{n_i})_i$ converges to a point of $Y$.

Compact operators are as similar to linear operators on finite-dimensional spaces as one has any right to expect from operators on infinite-dimensional spaces. 
In particular, these similarities show up in their spectral properties.

\begin{definition}
Suppose $X$ is a Banach space.
Then $B(X) \coloneq B(X,X)$ is not merely a Banach space but also an algebra:
If $S,T \in B(X)$, one defines $ST \in B(X)$ by
\(
(ST)x = S(T(x))
\), $x \in X$.
The inequality $\|ST\| \leq \|S\| \|T\|$ is easy to verify.
In particular, powers of $T \in B(X)$ can be defined:
$T^0 = \id$, the identity mapping on $X$, and $T^n = T^{n-1}T$ for $n \geq 1$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
An operator $T \in B(X)$ is said to be \emph{invertible} if there exists $S \in B(X)$ such that $ST = TS = \id$.
In this case, we write $S = T^{-1}$.
By the open mapping theorem, this happens if and only if $N(T) = \{0\}$ and $R(T) = X$.
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}\label{def:spectrum}
The \emph{spectrum} $\sigma(T)$ of an operator $T \in B(X)$ is the set of all scalars $\lambda \in \C$ such that $T - \lambda \id$ is not invertible.
Thus, $\lambda \in \sigma(T)$ if and only if at least one of the following statements is true:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item The range of $T - \lambda \id$ is not all of $X$.
\item $T - \lambda \id$ is not one-to-one (i.e., it is not injective, i.e. it has a non-trivial kernel). \label{item:not-one-to-one}
\end{enumerate}
If \ref{item:not-one-to-one} holds, $\lambda$ is said to be an \emph{eigenvalue} of $T$;
the corresponding \emph{eigenspace} is $N(T - \lambda \id)$;
each $x \in N(T - \lambda \id)$ except $x = 0$ is an \emph{eigenvector} of $T$ corresponding to $\lambda$;
it satisfies $Tx = \lambda x$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}
Let $X$ and $Y$ be Banach spaces.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item If $T \in B(X, Y)$ and $\dim R(T) < \infty$, then $T$ is compact.
  \item If $T \in B(X, Y)$, $T$ is compact, and $R(T)$ is closed, then $\dim R(T) < \infty$.
  \item The compact operators form a closed subspace of $B(X, Y)$ in its norm topology.
  \item If $T \in B(X)$, $T$ is compact, and $\lambda \neq 0$, then $\dim N(T - \lambda \id) < \infty$.
  \item If $\dim X = \infty$, $T \in B(X)$, and $T$ is compact, then $0 \in \sigma(T)$.
  \item If $S \in B(X)$, $T \in B(X)$, and $T$ is compact, so are $ST$ and $TS$.
  \qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}




\begin{definition}
\label{def:normal-hermitian-unitary-projection}
an operator $T \in B(\mathcal{H})$ is said to be
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \emph{normal} if $T^* T = T T^*$ \label{item:normal}
\item \emph{self-adjoint} (or \emph{Hermitian}) if $T^* = T$ \label{item:hermitian}
\item \emph{unitary} if $T^* T = \id = T T^*$, where $\id$ is the identity operator on $\mathcal{H}$ \label{item:unitary}
\item a \emph{projection} if $T^2 = T$ \label{item:projection}
\qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

It is clear that self-adjoint operators and unitary operators are normal.
Many results are about normal operators.
This algebraic requirement, namely that $T$ commutes with its adjoint, has strong analytic and geometric consequences.


\begin{theorem}
An operator $T \in B(\mathcal{H})$ is normal if and only if $\|Tx\| = \|T^* x\|$ for every $x \in \mathcal{H}$.
If $T$ is normal, then the following properties hold:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $N(T) = N(T^*)$
\item $R(T)$ is dense in $\mathcal{H}$ if and only if $T$ is one-to-one
\item $T$ is invertible if and only if there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\|Tx\| \geq \delta \|x\|$ for every $x \in \mathcal{H}$
\item If $Tx = \alpha x$ for some $x \in \mathcal{H}$ and $\alpha \in \C$, then $T^* x = \alpha^* x$
\item If $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are distinct eigenvalues of $T$, then the corresponding eigenspaces are orthogonal to each other.
\qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}


\begin{theorem}\label{thm:projection-properties}
Each of the following four properties of a projection $P \in B(\mathcal{H})$ (\autoref{def:normal-hermitian-unitary-projection}) implies the other three:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $P$ is self-adjoint
  \item $P$ is normal
  \item $R(P) = N(P)^\perp$
  \item $\braket{Px | x} = \|Px\|^2$ for every $x \in \mathcal{H}$
\end{itemize}
Moreover, two self-adjoint projections $P$ and $Q$ have $R(P) \perp R(Q)$ if and only if $PQ = 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{prop:orthogonal-projection-closed-subspace}
If $M \subseteq \mathcal H$ is a closed subspace, then there exists a unique self-adjoint projection $P \in B(\mathcal H)$ such that
\(
R(P)=M
\).
Moreover,
\(
N(P)=M^\perp
\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item If $U$ is unitary and $\lambda \in \sigma(U)$, then $|\lambda| = 1$.
\item If $S$ is hermitian (self-adjoint) and $\lambda \in \sigma(S)$, then $\lambda$ is real.
\qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}



\begin{definition}\label{def:resolution-of-identity}
Let $\mathfrak{M}$ be a $\sigma$-algebra on a set $\Omega$, and let $\mathcal{H}$ be a Hilbert space.
A \hl{\emph{resolution of the identity}} (on $\mathfrak{M}$) is a map %mapping
\[
E \colon \mathfrak{M} \to B(\mathcal{H})
\]
with the following properties:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $E(\emptyset) = 0$, $E(\Omega) = \id$ \label{item:Eemptyset-zero-EOmega-identity}
\item Each $E(\omega)$ is a self-adjoint projection (see \autoref{def:normal-hermitian-unitary-projection}) \label{item:each-Eomega-self-adjoint-projection}
\item $E(\omega_1 \cap \omega_2) = E(\omega_1) E(\omega_2)$ \label{item:Eomega1capomega2-is-Eomega1-times-Eomega2}
\item If $\omega_1 \cap \omega_2 = \emptyset$, then $E(\omega_1 \cup \omega_2) = E(\omega_1) + E(\omega_2)$ \label{item:if-disjoint-then-Eomega1cupomega2-is-sum}
\item For every $x,y \in \mathcal{H}$, the set function $E_{x,y}$ defined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:complex-measure-via-inner-product}
E_{x,y}(\omega) = \braket{E(\omega)x | y}
\end{equation}
is a complex measure on $\mathfrak{M}$. \label{item:resolution-of-identity-complex-measure-via-inner-product}
\qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}
When $\mathfrak{M}$ is the $\sigma$-algebra of all Borel sets on a compact or locally compact Hausdorff space, it is customary to add another requirement to \ref{item:resolution-of-identity-complex-measure-via-inner-product}:
Each $E_{x,y}$ should be a \emph{regular} Borel measure (this is automatically satisfied on compact metric spaces).


Some immediate consequences of the properties in \autoref{def:resolution-of-identity}:
\begin{itemize}
\item 
  By \ref{item:each-Eomega-self-adjoint-projection} and \ref{item:resolution-of-identity-complex-measure-via-inner-product}, we have
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:Exx-positive-measure}
  E_{x,x}(\omega) 
  = 
  \braket{E(\omega)x | x} 
  =
  \braket{E(\omega)^2 x | x}
  =
  \braket{E(\omega) x | E(\omega) x}
  =
  \|E(\omega)x\|^2
  \end{equation}
  for every $x \in \mathcal{H}$ so that each $E_{x,x}$ is a positive measure on $\mathfrak{M}$ with total variation $\|E_{x,x}\| = E_{x,x}(\Omega) = \|x\|^2$.\footnote{In particular, if $\|x\| = 1$, then $E_{x,x}$ is a probability measure on $\mathfrak{M}$, which will be important in \autoref{sec:observables}.}
\item 
  By \ref{item:Eomega1capomega2-is-Eomega1-times-Eomega2}, any two of the projections $E(\omega)$ commute with each other.
\item 
  If $\omega_1 \cap \omega_2 = \emptyset$, \ref{item:Eemptyset-zero-EOmega-identity} and \ref{item:Eomega1capomega2-is-Eomega1-times-Eomega2} show that the ranges of $E(\omega_1)$ and $E(\omega_2)$ are orthogonal to each other (\autoref{thm:projection-properties}).
\end{itemize}


\begin{proposition}
If $E$ is a resolution of the identity, and if $x \in \mathcal{H}$, then
\[
\omega \mapsto E(\omega)x
\]
is countably additive $\mathcal{H}$-valued measure on $\mathfrak{M}$.
\end{proposition}


Next we tackle the integration of functions with respect to the projection-valued measures defined in \autoref{def:resolution-of-identity}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:integration-projection-valued-measure}
If $E$ is a resolution of the identity, then there exists an isometric *-isomorphism 
$
\Psi
$
of the Banach algebra $L^\infty(E)$ onto a closed normal subalgebra $\mathcal{A}$ of $B(\mathcal{H})$, which is related to $E$ by
\[
\braket{\Psi(f)x | y} = \int_\Omega f \,\dif E_{x,y}
\]
for every $f \in L^\infty(E)$ and every $x,y \in \mathcal{H}$.
This justifies the notation $\Psi(f) = \int_\Omega f \,\dif E$.
Moreover,
\[
\|\Psi(f)x\|^2 = \int_\Omega |f|^2 \,\dif E_{x,x}
\]
for every $f \in L^\infty(E)$ and every $x \in \mathcal{H}$
and
an operator $Q \in B(\mathcal{H})$ commutes with every $E(\omega)$ if and only if $Q$ commutes with every $\Psi(f)$.

Recall that a \emph{normal} subalgebra $\mathcal{A}$ of $B(\mathcal{H})$ is a commutative one which contains $T^*$ for every $T \in \mathcal{A}$.
To say that $\Psi$ is a *-isomorphism means that $\Psi$ is one-to-one, linear, multiplicative and that $\Psi(f^*) = \Psi(f)^*$ for every $f \in L^\infty(E)$.
\end{theorem}


The principal assertion of \autoref{thm:spectral-theorem} is that every bounded normal operator $T$ on a Hilbert space induces (in a canonical way) a resolution $E$ of the identity on the Borel subsets of its spectrum $\sigma(T)$ and that $T$ can be reconstructed from $E$ by an integral of the type discussed in \autoref{thm:integration-projection-valued-measure}. 
A large part of the theory of normal operators depends on this fact.

\begin{theorem}[Spectral theorem]\label{thm:spectral-theorem}
If $T \in B(\mathcal{H})$ and $T$ is normal (\autoref{def:normal-hermitian-unitary-projection}), then there exists a unique resolution of the identity $E$ (\autoref{def:resolution-of-identity}) on the Borel subsets of $\sigma(T)$ (\autoref{def:spectrum}) such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-theorem}
% \boxed{
T = \int_{\sigma(T)} \lambda \,\dif E(\lambda)
% }
\end{equation}
% where 
% \[
% \sigma(T) = \{\lambda \in \C : T - \lambda \id \text{ is not invertible}\}
% \]
% is the spectrum of $T$.
Moreover, every projection $E(\omega)$ commutes with every $S \in B(\mathcal{H})$ which commutes with $T$.
\end{theorem}

We call $E$ the \emph{spectral decomposition} of $T$.

Sometimes it is convenient to think of $E$  as being defined for all Borel sets in $\C$;
to achieve this put $E(\omega) = 0$ if $\omega \cap \sigma(T) = \emptyset$.



\begin{notation}[Symbolic Calculus for Normal Operators]
If $E$ is the spectral decomposition of a normal operator $T \in B(\mathcal{H})$, and if $f$ is a bounded Borel function on $\sigma(T)$, it is customary to denote the operator
\(
\Psi(f) = \int_{\sigma(T)} f \,\dif E
\)
by $f(T)$.
\end{notation}
% Using this notation, parts of \autoref{thm:integration-projection-valued-measure} and \autoref{thm:spectral-theorem} can be summarized as follows:


\begin{theorem}\label{thm:normal-bounded-operator-spectral-properties}
A normal $T \in B(\mathcal{H})$ is 
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item hermitian if and only if $\sigma(T)$ lies on the real axis
\item unitary if and only if $\sigma(T)$ lies on the unit circle
\qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}












\subsubsection{Unbounded operators}

Let $\mathcal H$ be a Hilbert space.
By an \emph{operator} in $\mathcal H$ we mean a linear map
\(
T \colon D(T) \to \mathcal H
\),
where the \emph{domain} $D(T)$ is a linear subspace of $\mathcal H$.
% Its \emph{range} is
% \(
% R(T) \coloneq \{Tx : x \in D(T)\}
% \).
If $D(T)$ is dense in $\mathcal H$, we say that $T$ is \hl{\emph{densely defined}}.

% \begin{definition}
The \emph{graph} of an operator $T$ is
\(
G(T) \coloneq \{(x,Tx) : x \in D(T)\} \subseteq \mathcal H \times \mathcal H
\).
The operator $T$ is said to be \emph{closed} if $G(T)$ is closed in $\mathcal H \times \mathcal H$.
It is said to be \emph{closable} if the closure of $G(T)$ is itself the graph of an operator.
% \end{definition}

Equivalently, $T$ is closed if whenever $x_n \in D(T)$, $x_n \to x$ in $\mathcal H$, and $Tx_n \to y$ in $\mathcal H$, then $x \in D(T)$ and $Tx = y$.

% \begin{definition}
Suppose $T$ is densely defined.
An element $y \in \mathcal H$ is said to lie in the domain of the \emph{adjoint} $T^*$ if the map
\(
x \mapsto \braket{Tx | y}
\),
\(
x \in D(T)
\),
is a bounded linear functional on $D(T)$ (with the norm inherited from $\mathcal H$).
By Hahn-Banach and the Riesz representation theorem, there is then a unique vector $T^*y \in \mathcal H$ such that
\[
\braket{Tx | y} = \braket{x | T^*y}
\]
for every $x \in D(T)$.
Thus
\[
D(T^*) \coloneq \left\{y \in \mathcal H : x \mapsto \braket{Tx | y}
\text{ is bounded on } D(T)\right\}
\]
and $T^* \colon D(T^*) \to \mathcal H$ is an operator in $\mathcal H$.
% \end{definition}
If $T \in B(\mathcal H)$, this agrees with the usual adjoint of a bounded operator.

\begin{definition}\label{def:symmetric-self-adjoint-unbounded}
A densely defined operator $T$ is called
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item 
\emph{symmetric} if
\(
\braket{Tx | y} = \braket{x | Ty}
\)
for every $x,y \in D(T)$, or,
equivalently,
\(
T \subseteq T^*
\)
\item \label{item:densely-defined-self-adjoint}
\emph{self-adjoint} if
\(
T = T^*
\)
\qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}


Unlike for bounded operators, symmetry and self-adjointness are not the same for unbounded operators.

% \begin{definition}
If $S$ and $T$ are operators in $\mathcal H$, their sum and product are defined on the natural domains
\[
D(S+T) = D(S)\cap D(T)
\qquad
D(ST) = \{x \in D(T) : Tx \in D(S)\}
\]
% \end{definition}
With these definitions, the associative laws remain valid, but one must always watch domains carefully.

\begin{theorem}
If $T$ is densely defined, then $T^*$ is closed.
In particular, every self-adjoint operator is closed.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}
If $T$ is densely defined and closed, then 
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item
$D(T^*)$ is dense in $\mathcal H$ and
\(
T^{**} = T
\).
\item
\(
I + T^*T
\)
is a one-to-one mapping of
\(
D(T^*T)=\{x\in D(T): Tx\in D(T^*)\}
\)
onto $\mathcal H$.
Its inverse
\(
(I+T^*T)^{-1}
\)
belongs to $B(\mathcal H)$.
\item
$T^*T$ is self-adjoint and positive, i.e.,
\(
\braket{T^*Tx | x} = \|Tx\|^2 \ge 0
\)
for all $x \in D(T^*T)$.
\qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{definition}\label{def:spectrum-unbounded-operator}
The \emph{resolvent set} of an operator $T$ is the set of all $\lambda \in \mathbb C$ such that
\(
T-\lambda I
\)
is one-to-one, maps $D(T)$ onto $\mathcal H$, and has bounded inverse in $B(\mathcal H)$.
Its complement is the \emph{spectrum} $\sigma(T)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}\label{def:domain-of-Psi}
Let $E$ be a resolution of the identity on a measurable space $(\Omega,\mathfrak M)$, and let
\(
f \colon \Omega \to \mathbb C
\)
be measurable.
Define
\(
D_f \coloneq \{x \in \mathcal H : \int_\Omega |f|^2 \, \dif E_{x,x} < \infty \}
\).
Then $D_f$ is a dense subspace of $\mathcal H$.
The operator $\Psi(f)$ is defined by the requirement
\[
\braket{\Psi(f)x | y}
=
\int_\Omega f \, \dif E_{x,y}
\]
for every $x \in D_f$ and every $y \in \mathcal H$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}%[Functional calculus for measurable functions]
With the notation above, $\Psi(f)$ is a densely defined closed operator with domain $D_f$, and
\[
\|\Psi(f)x\|^2 = \int_\Omega |f|^2\, \dif E_{x,x}
\]
for every $x \in D_f$.
If $f$ and $g$ are measurable, then
\(
\Psi(f)\Psi(g) \subseteq \Psi(fg)
\),
and
\(
\Psi(f)^* = \Psi(f^*)
\).
Hence
\(
\Psi(f)^*\Psi(f)=\Psi(|f|^2)=\Psi(f)\Psi(f)^*
\).
So every operator of the form $\Psi(f)$ is normal.
\end{theorem}


\begin{definition}\label{def:normal-operator}
A (not necessarily bounded) linear operator $T$ in $\mathcal{H}$ is said to be \hl[2]{\emph{normal}} if $T$ is closed, densely defined, and $T^* T = T T^*$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}
If $N$ is normal, then
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $D(N)=D(N^*)$
\item $\|Nx\|=\|N^*x\|$ for every $x\in D(N)$
\item $N$ is \emph{maximally normal}: if $M$ is normal and $N\subseteq M$, then $M=N$
\qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}




\autoref{thm:normal-bounded-operator-spectral-properties} can be extended to unbounded normal operators as follows:

\begin{theorem}
A densely defined normal operator $T$ is 
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item self-adjoint if and only if $\sigma(T)$ lies on the real axis
\item unitary if and only if $\sigma(T)$ lies on the unit circle \label{item:unitary-if-spectrum-on-unit-circle}
\end{enumerate}
Note that if \ref{item:unitary-if-spectrum-on-unit-circle} holds, then necessarily $D(T)=\mathcal H$, so $T$ is bounded.
\end{theorem}


\begin{notation}
If \(M \subseteq \mathcal H\) is a closed subspace, we write \(P_M\) for the orthogonal projection onto \(M\), i.e. the unique self-adjoint projection with range \(R(P_M)=M\) (see \autoref{prop:orthogonal-projection-closed-subspace}).
\end{notation}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectral-projection-eigenspace}
Let \(A\) be self-adjoint with spectral measure \(E_A\). Then for every eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of \(A\),
\[
E_A(\{\lambda\}) = P_{N(A-\lambda \id)}
\]
where \(P_{N(A-\lambda \id)}\) is the orthogonal projection onto the eigenspace
\[
N(A-\lambda \id)=\{x\in\mathcal H:Ax=\lambda x\}
\]
This notation is justified by \autoref{prop:orthogonal-projection-closed-subspace}, since eigenspaces of self-adjoint operators are closed subspaces.
% In particular, if \(\lambda\) is nondegenerate with normalized eigenvector \(\varphi\), then
% \[
% E_A(\{\lambda\}) = P_{\mathrm{span}\{\varphi\}} = P_\varphi
% \qedhere
% \]
\end{proposition}


\begin{theorem}[Spectral theorem]\label{thm:spectral-theorem-unbounded}
Every normal operator $T$ in $\mathcal H$ has a unique spectral decomposition\footnote{%
In the unbounded case, a resolution of the identity means exactly the same thing as before (\autoref{def:resolution-of-identity}): a projection-valued measure
\(
E:\mathfrak M\to B(\mathcal H)
\).
What is new is that for measurable \(f\), the operator
\(
\Psi(f)
\)
need not be bounded; its domain is
\(
D_f=\{x\in\mathcal H:\int |f|^2\,\dif E_{x,x}<\infty\}
\)
(see \autoref{def:domain-of-Psi}).%
}  $E$, such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inner-product-spectral-representation}
% \boxed{
\braket{Tx | y} = \int_{\sigma(T)} \lambda \,\dif E_{x,y}(\lambda)
% }
\end{equation}
for every $x \in D(T) \overset{\text{\ref{def:domain-of-Psi}}}{=} \{x\in\mathcal H:\int_{\sigma(T)} |\lambda|^2\,\dif E_{x,x}(\lambda)<\infty\}$ and every $y \in \mathcal H$.
Equivalently:
\[
% \boxed{
T=\int_{\sigma(T)} \lambda\, \dif E(\lambda)
% }
\]
Moreover, $E(\omega) S = S E(\omega)$ for every Borel set $\omega \subseteq \sigma(T)$ and every $S \in B(\mathcal H)$ which commutes with $T$ in the sense that $ST \subseteq TS$.
\end{theorem}














\subsection{Postulates} \label{sec:postulates-of-quantum-mechanics}

\subsubsection{The state}

To an isolated quantum system we associate a complex Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$.
A \emph{state} of the system is represented by a normalized vector
\(
\psi \in \mathcal{H}
\)
with
\(
\|\psi\| = 1
\).
If $\mathcal{H}$ is realized as a space of wave functions, for example $\mathcal{H} = L^2(\R^3)$, then, as already introduced in \eqref{eq:inner-product}, the scalar product is
\[
\braket{\psi_1 | \psi_2}
\coloneq
\int_{\R^3} \psi_1(\vect{x}, t)^* \, \psi_2(\vect{x}, t) \,\dif \vect{x}
\]
and the normalization condition becomes
\(
\|\psi\|^2
=
\braket{\psi | \psi}
=
\int_{\R^3} |\psi(\vect{x}, t)|^2 \,\dif \vect{x}
=
1
\).
In that case, $|\psi(\vect{x}, t)|^2$ is interpreted as probability density.

The linear structure of $\mathcal{H}$ gives the \emph{superposition principle}:
if $\psi_1,\psi_2 \in \mathcal{H}$ and $\alpha,\beta \in \C$, then also
\(
\alpha \psi_1 + \beta \psi_2 \in \mathcal{H}
\).
If, in addition,
\(
\|\alpha \psi_1 + \beta \psi_2\| = 1
\),
then this superposition again represents a physical state.
Thus quantum states behave like waves: linear combinations of states are again states.

\subsubsection{The time evolution}

As already introduced in \eqref{eq:schroedinger}, the time evolution of a quantum state is governed by the Schrödinger equation
\[
\boxed{
\iu \hbar  \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi(\vect{x}, t) = H  \psi(\vect{x}, t)
}
\]
where $H$ is the Hamiltonian operator \eqref{eq:hamiltonian}.
It is linear: any linear combination of solutions is again a solution.

We assume the Hamiltonian $H$ to be self-adjoint (see Definition \ref{def:hermitian}, \ref{def:normal-hermitian-unitary-projection} \ref{item:hermitian}, \ref{def:symmetric-self-adjoint-unbounded}).
If $H$ is time-independent, then the solutions can be written as
\[
\psi(t) = \eu^{- \iu H t / \hbar} \psi(0)
\]
Thus the time evolution operator is
\(
U(t) = \eu^{- \iu H t / \hbar}
\).
As discussed before, $\eu^{\iu A}$ is unitary whenever $A$ is Hermitian, so $U(t)$ is unitary (see Definition \ref{def:unitary}, \ref{def:normal-hermitian-unitary-projection} \ref{item:unitary}).
Hence time evolution preserves the inner product and in particular the norm:
if \(\braket{\psi(0) | \psi(0)} = 1\), then
\(
\braket{\psi(t) | \psi(t)} = 1
\)
for all $t \in \R$.







\subsubsection{Observables} \label{sec:observables}

A measurable physical quantity is represented by an observable, that is, by a self-adjoint operator
\(
A = A^*
\)
(see Definitions \ref{def:hermitian}, \ref{def:normal-hermitian-unitary-projection} \ref{item:hermitian}, \ref{def:symmetric-self-adjoint-unbounded}, Theorems \ref{thm:spectral-theorem}, \ref{thm:spectral-theorem-unbounded}).
The expectation value of $A$ in a state $\psi$ is
\[
\braket{A}_{\psi}
=
\braket{\psi | A | \psi}
\coloneq
\braket{\psi | A\psi}
\]
provided $\psi \in D(A)$.
The possible outcomes of a measurement of $A$ are given by the spectrum (see \autoref{def:spectrum-unbounded-operator}) $\sigma(A) = \sigma_p(A) \cup \sigma_c(A)$ (for self-adjoint (wether bounded or unbounded) operators, the residual spectrum $\sigma_r(A)$ is always empty) of $A$.

% This is the calculation one would like to preserve in general Hilbert spaces.
% The difficulty is that in infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces a self-adjoint operator need not admit an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors: there may be continuous spectrum 

In a general Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$, every self-adjoint operator $A$ admits a spectral decomposition
(see \autoref{sec:operators}, \autoref{def:spectrum-unbounded-operator} and \autoref{thm:spectral-theorem-unbounded}).
Denote the spectrum of $A$ by $\sigma_A \coloneq \sigma(A)$.
By \autoref{thm:spectral-theorem-unbounded}, for every self-adjoint operator there exists a unique projection-valued measure
\[
E_A \colon \mathcal B(\sigma_A) \to B(\mathcal H) % why bounded? because each E_A(omega) is a projection, hence a bounded operator
\]
on the Borel sets of $\sigma_A$ such that
\(
A = \int_{\sigma_A} \lambda \,\dif E_A(\lambda)
\).
Here each $E_A(\omega)$ is a self-adjoint projection, and by \autoref{def:resolution-of-identity} and the remarks following it,
\[
\braket{\psi|E_A(\omega)\psi} \overset{\text{\eqref{eq:Exx-positive-measure}}}{=} \|E_A(\omega)\psi\|^2 \ge 0
\]
for every Borel set $\omega \subseteq \sigma_A$.
Moreover,
\(
E_A(\sigma_A)=\id
\) and
\(
E_A(\emptyset)=0
\),
so for normalized $\psi$ we have
\(
\braket{\psi|E_A(\sigma_A)\psi} = \braket{\psi|\psi} = 1
\).
Therefore, 
for every normalized state $\psi$,
the map
\begin{equation}\label{eq:probability-measure-from-spectral-decomposition}
\mu_\psi^A(\omega)
\coloneq
\braket{\psi|E_A(\omega)\psi}
\overset{\text{\ref{def:resolution-of-identity}.\ref{item:each-Eomega-self-adjoint-projection}}}{=}
\braket{E_A(\omega)\psi|\psi}
\overset{\text{\eqref{eq:complex-measure-via-inner-product}}}{=}
E_{\psi,\psi}(\omega)
\end{equation}
for $\omega \in \mathcal{B}(\sigma_A)$, 
defines a probability measure $\Prob_A^\psi(\cdot) \coloneq \mu_\psi^A(\cdot)$ on $\sigma_A$.
This is the probability distribution of the measurement outcomes of $A$ in the state $\psi$.
This is called the \emph{Born rule}.

% So the generalization of the discrete formula
% \(
% p_i = \bigl|\braket{\varphi_i|\psi}\bigr|^2
% \)
% is not, in general, a pdf, but a probability measure
% \[
% \Prob_A^\psi(B)
% =
% \mu_\psi^A(B)
% =
% \braket{\psi|E_A(B)\psi}
% \]
% for Borel sets $B \in \mathcal B(\sigma_A)$.
% This is the precise infinite-dimensional analogue of the Born rule.

% \[
% \begin{aligned}
% \braket{A}_{\psi}
% &=
% \braket{\psi|A\psi}
% \\
% &=
% \Braket{\psi | \int_{\sigma_A} \lambda \,\dif E_A(\lambda)\, \psi}
% \\
% &=
% \int_{\sigma_A} \lambda \, \dif \braket{\psi|E_A(\lambda)\psi}
% \\
% &=
% \int_{\sigma_A} \lambda \, \dif \mu_\psi^A(\lambda)
% \end{aligned}
% \]

With this notation, the expectation value is obtained as 
\[
\begin{aligned}
\braket{A}_{\psi}
&=
\braket{\psi|A\psi}
\overset{\text{\ref{def:symmetric-self-adjoint-unbounded}.\ref{item:densely-defined-self-adjoint}}}{=}
\braket{A\psi|\psi}
\\
&\overset{\text{\eqref{eq:inner-product-spectral-representation}}}{=}
\int_{\sigma_A} \lambda \,\dif E_{\psi,\psi}(\lambda)
% \\
% &\overset{\text{\eqref{eq:complex-measure-via-inner-product}}}{=}
% \int_{\sigma_A} \lambda \,\dif \braket{E_A(\lambda)\psi | \psi}
\\
&\overset{\text{\eqref{eq:probability-measure-from-spectral-decomposition}}}{=}
\int_{\sigma_A} \lambda \,\dif \mu_\psi^A(\lambda)
\end{aligned}
\]
% where we used notational shorthand `$\dif \braket{\psi|E_A(\lambda)\psi} = \dif \braket{E_A(\,\cdot\,)\psi | \psi}(\lambda)$' in the penultimate step.
which is precisely the probabilistic expectation of the measurement outcomes with respect to the spectral probability measure.
In case the spectrum has both discrete and continuous parts, then $\mu_\psi^A$ automatically contains both.

% If the measure happens to be absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure, then one may write
% \[
% \mu_\psi^A(B) = \int_B \rho_\psi^A(\lambda)\,\dif\lambda
% \]
% for some density $\rho_\psi^A$.
% But pedagogically the fundamental object is the measure $\mu_\psi^A$, not necessarily a density.

After an ideal measurement, the state updates by projection.
If one only knows that the outcome lies in a Borel set $\omega$, then the post-measurement state is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:post-measurement-state}
\psi
\longmapsto
\frac{E_A(\omega)\psi}{\|E_A(\omega)\psi\|}
\end{equation}
provided $E_A(\omega)\psi \neq 0$.

\begin{example}[Finite-dimensional case]\label{ex:finite-dimensional-case}
If ${A} \colon \C^n \to \C^n$ is self-adjoint, then by the (finite-dimensional) spectral theorem there exist distinct real eigenvalues
\(
\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_r
\)
and pairwise orthogonal eigenspaces
\[
M_j \coloneq N({A}-\lambda_j \id)
\]
for
\(
j=1,\dots,r
\),
such that
\[
\C^n = \bigoplus_{j=1}^r M_j
\]
and
\[
\sigma(A)=\{\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_r\}
\]

Let $E_A$ be the spectral measure of $A$ from \autoref{thm:spectral-theorem-unbounded}. Since the spectrum is finite, the spectral integral
% \(
% A = \int_{\sigma(A)} \lambda \,\dif E_A(\lambda)
% \)
reduces to a finite sum over the atoms $\{\lambda_j\}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:discrete-spectrum-spectral-integral}
A
=
\int_{\sigma(A)} \lambda \,\dif E_A(\lambda)
=
\sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j\, E_A(\{\lambda_j\})
\end{equation}
By \autoref{prop:spectral-projection-eigenspace},
\(
E_A(\{\lambda_j\}) = P_{M_j}
\),
where $P_{M_j}$ denotes the orthogonal projection onto the eigenspace $M_j$.
Hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:discrete-spectrum-spectral-decomposition}
{A} = \sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j P_{M_j}
\end{equation}
If we choose an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors in each eigenspace \(M_j\),
\[
\{\varphi_{j,k}: 1\le j\le r,\ 1\le k\le d_j\}
\]
with
\(
d_j = \dim M_j
\),
\(
M_j = \mathrm{span}\{\varphi_{j,1},\dots,\varphi_{j,d_j}\}
\),
 the orthogonal projection onto \(M_j\) is the sum of the one-dimensional projections onto these basis vectors. Hence
\[
P_{M_j}\psi 
= % why?
\sum_{k=1}^{d_j} P_{\varphi_{j,k}}\psi
= % why?
\sum_{k=1}^{d_j} \braket{\varphi_{j,k}|\psi} \varphi_{j,k}
\]


Substituting \eqref{eq:discrete-spectrum-spectral-decomposition} into the expectation value gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
\braket{{A}}_{\psi}
&=
\braket{\psi|{A}\psi}
\\
&=
\left\langle \psi \middle| \sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j P_{M_j}\psi \right\rangle
\\
&=
\sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j \, \braket{\psi|P_{M_j}\psi}
\\
&=
\sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j \, \|P_{M_j}\psi\|^2
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus the numbers
\(
p_j \coloneq \|P_{M_j}\psi\|^2
\)
are precisely the probabilities of obtaining the outcomes $\lambda_j$.
So in the finite-dimensional case the expectation value has the familiar form
\(
\braket{{A}}_{\psi} = \sum_{j = 1}^r \lambda_j p_j
\).


The general formulas from the main text hence reduce to these familiar finite-dimensional discrete ones. In particular, \eqref{eq:discrete-spectrum-spectral-integral} shows explicitly how the spectral integral collapses to a finite sum, and \autoref{prop:spectral-projection-eigenspace} identifies the spectral projections with the orthogonal projections onto the eigenspaces.
Indeed, given a Borel set $\omega \subseteq \sigma(A)$, we have
\begin{equation}\label{eq:EAomega-discrete-spectrum}
E_A(\omega) 
=
E_A\left(\bigcup_{\smash[b]{\lambda_j} \in \omega} \{\lambda_j\}\right)
\overset{\text{\ref{def:resolution-of-identity}.\ref{item:if-disjoint-then-Eomega1cupomega2-is-sum}}}{=} 
\sum_{\lambda_j \in \omega} E_A(\{\lambda_j\})
\overset{\text{\ref{prop:spectral-projection-eigenspace}}}{=}
\sum_{\lambda_j \in \omega} P_{M_j}
\end{equation}
Here the sum is finite because $\sigma({A})=\{\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_r\}$ is finite and hence $\omega \subseteq \sigma(A)$ is so as well.
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mu_\psi^{A}(\omega)
&\overset{\text{\eqref{eq:probability-measure-from-spectral-decomposition}}}{=}
\braket{\psi|E_{A}(\omega)\psi}
\\
&\overset{\text{\eqref{eq:EAomega-discrete-spectrum}}}{=}
\left\langle \psi \middle| \sum_{\smash[b]{\lambda_j} \in \omega} P_{M_j}\psi \right\rangle
\\
&=
\sum_{\lambda_j \in \omega} \braket{\psi|P_{M_j}\psi}
\\
&\overset{\text{\ref{thm:projection-properties}}}{=}
\sum_{\lambda_j \in \omega} \|P_{M_j}\psi\|^2 % please simplify further in terms of inner product etc, as discussed...
\end{aligned}
\]
which recovers exactly the usual formula.
In particular,
\(
\Prob_{A}^\psi(\{\lambda_j\}) = \|P_{M_j}\psi\|^2
\).

If all eigenvalues are nondegenerate, then each $M_j$ is one-dimensional, say
\[
M_j=\mathrm{span}\{\varphi_j\}
\]
and the corresponding projection becomes
\(
P_{M_j}=P_{\varphi_j}
\),
where
\(
P_{\varphi_j}\psi = \braket{\varphi_j|\psi}\,\varphi_j
\).
In that case, we have
\(
\Prob_{A}^{\smash[t]{\psi}}(\{\lambda_j\}) = |\braket{\varphi_j|\psi}|^2
\).
Moreover, if $\omega=\{\lambda_j\}$ and $\braket{\varphi_j|\psi}\neq 0$, then \eqref{eq:post-measurement-state} reduces to
\[
\psi
\longmapsto
\frac{P_{\varphi_j}\psi}{\|P_{\varphi_j}\psi\|}
=
\varphi_j \, \eu^{\iu \theta}
\]
for some phase $\theta \in [0,2\pi)$.
\end{example}

\subsubsection{Joint systems and composition}

If two quantum systems are described by Hilbert spaces \(\mathcal H_A\) and \(\mathcal H_B\), then the composite system is described by the tensor product Hilbert space
\(
\mathcal H_A \otimes \mathcal H_B
\).

The tensor product is linear in each factor, so it is compatible with the superposition principle: superpositions in the subsystems induce corresponding superpositions in the composite system.

If the two subsystems are in pure states \(\psi_A \in \mathcal H_A\) and \(\psi_B \in \mathcal H_B\), then the corresponding product state of the joint system is
\[
\psi_A \otimes \psi_B \in \mathcal H_A \otimes \mathcal H_B
\]

More generally, the composite Hilbert space also contains superpositions of product states. Not every state in \(\mathcal H_A \otimes \mathcal H_B\) can be written in the form \(\psi_A \otimes \psi_B\). States that cannot be written in this form are called \emph{entangled}.


\subsubsection{Abstraction and simplification}

From now on, we restrict to the case where the Hilbert space is finite-dimensional.
Then
\[
\mathcal{H} \cong \C^n
\]
for some $n \in \N$, i.e. $\mathcal{H}$ is isomorphic to the standard $n$-dimensional complex vector space.  

This allows us to work with vectors and matrices instead of general Hilbert-space operators and spectral measures.

With respect to a fixed orthonormal basis, a vector $\psi \in \mathcal{H} \cong \C^n$ is represented by a column vector
\[
\ket{\psi}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\psi_1 \\
% \psi_2 \\
\vdots \\
\psi_n
\end{bmatrix}
\]
called a \emph{ket}.
Its adjoint is the row vector
\[
\bra{\psi}
=
\ket{\psi}^*
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\psi_1^*,
% \psi_2^*  &
\ldots, 
\psi_n^* 
\end{bmatrix}
\]
called a \emph{bra}.
The scalar product becomes
\[
\braket{\psi_{A} | \psi_{B}}
=
\bra{\psi_{A}}  \ket{\psi_{B}}
=
\sum_{i=1}^n \psi_{A,i}^* \psi_{B,i}
\in \C
\]
% which is the usual matrix multiplication of a row vector with a column vector.

% Projectors onto one-dimensional subspaces can be written as
% \[
% P_{\varphi} = \ket{\varphi}\bra{\varphi}
% \]
% because
% \[
% \ket{\varphi}\bra{\varphi}\ket{\psi}
% =
% \braket{\varphi|\psi}\ket{\varphi}
% \]

% Thus, if
% \[
% A = \sum_i \lambda_i P_{\varphi_i}
% \]
% then
% \[
% A = \sum_i \lambda_i \ket{\varphi_i}\bra{\varphi_i}
% \]

The notation is also useful for expressing Hermitian observables.
As seen in \autoref{ex:finite-dimensional-case}, if $A$ is self-adjoint, we have
\[
% \begin{aligned}
A
=
\sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j P_{M_j} 
=
\sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j \sum_{k=1}^{d_j} P_{\varphi_{j,k}}
% \end{aligned}
\]
and therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
\ket{A \psi}
% &=
% \Ket{\sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j \sum_{k=1}^{d_j} P_{\varphi_{j,k}} \psi} \\
&=
\sum_{\smash[b]{j}=1}^r \lambda_j \sum_{k=1}^{d_j} \Ket{P_{\varphi_{j,k}} \psi} \\
% &=
% \sum_{\smash[b]{j}=1}^r \lambda_j \sum_{k=1}^{d_j} \Ket{\braket{\varphi_{j,k}|\psi} \varphi_{j,k}} \\
&=
\sum_{\smash[b]{j}=1}^r \lambda_j \sum_{k=1}^{d_j} \braket{\varphi_{j,k}|\psi} \ket{\varphi_{j,k}} \\
&=
\sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j \sum_{k=1}^{d_j} \ket{\varphi_{j,k}}\bra{\varphi_{j,k}} \ket{\psi}
\end{aligned}
\]
whereby we obtained the common way to represent projectors as
\(
P_{\varphi_{\smash[b]{j,k}}} = \ket{\varphi_{j,k}}\bra{\varphi_{j,k}}
\)
and thus the observable $A$ itself as $A = \sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j \sum_{k=1}^{\smash{d_j}} \ket{\varphi_{j,k}}\bra{\varphi_{j,k}}$.


More generally, if $\{\ket{k}\}_k$ is an orthonormal basis, then every operator $A$ can be expanded as
\[
A = \sum_{k,l} \braket{k|A|l} \ket{k}\bra{l}
\]

A useful identity is the resolution of the identity
\[
\id = \sum_i \ket{i}\bra{i}
\]
which can be inserted into calculations at convenient places.  % [oai_citation:5‡book_Invitation-to-Quantum-Informatics pages 52 - 59.pdf](sediment://file_00000000b410720abc2d96070c66ef5d)  % [oai_citation:6‡Note 17 Feb 2027.pdf](sediment://file_00000000ec74724390e9a998a2de5991)

\subsubsection{The trace}

For an operator $A \in \operatorname{End}(\mathcal{H})$ on a finite-dimensional Hilbert space, the trace is defined by
\[
\Trace(A)
\coloneq
\sum_k \braket{k|A|k}
\]
where $\{\ket{k}\}_k$ is any orthonormal basis.

The trace is basis-independent.
A key identity is
\[
\Trace(AB) = \Trace(BA)
\]

Hence for a unitary change of basis $U$,
\[
\Trace(UAU^*)
=
\Trace(U^*UA)
=
\Trace(A)
\]

For a pure state $\ket{\psi}$ and an observable $A$, the expectation value can be written as
\[
\langle A \rangle_{\psi}
=
\braket{\psi|A|\psi}
=
\Trace\bigl(\ket{\psi}\bra{\psi}A\bigr)
\]
which is the form that extends naturally to mixed states.  % [oai_citation:7‡book_Invitation-to-Quantum-Informatics pages 52 - 59.pdf](sediment://file_00000000b410720abc2d96070c66ef5d)  % [oai_citation:8‡Note 17 Feb 2027.pdf](sediment://file_00000000ec74724390e9a998a2de5991)

\subsubsection{Density matrices}

So far, states were pure states.
More generally, one may only know a probability distribution over pure states.
This describes a \emph{statistical mixture}, not a superposition.

Such a state is represented by a density operator $\rho$.

In finite dimension, a density matrix is a positive trace-one operator:
\[
\rho = \rho^*
\qquad
\rho \geq 0
\qquad
\Trace(\rho) = 1
\]

Equivalently, by the spectral theorem,
\[
\rho = \sum_i \lambda_i \ket{\varphi_i}\bra{\varphi_i}
\]
with
\[
\lambda_i \geq 0
\qquad
\sum_i \lambda_i = 1
\]

Thus the eigenvalues of $\rho$ may be interpreted as probabilities.

Pure states are exactly those density matrices of the form
\[
\rho = \ket{\psi}\bra{\psi}
\]
for some normalized $\psi$.

The time evolution of density matrices is
\[
\rho(t) = U(t)\rho(0)U(t)^*
\]

If
\[
A = \sum_i \lambda_i \ket{\varphi_i}\bra{\varphi_i}
\]
is an observable, then the probability of obtaining the outcome $\lambda_i$ is
\[
\Prob(\lambda_i)
=
\Trace\bigl(\ket{\varphi_i}\bra{\varphi_i}\rho\bigr)
\]

The expectation value of $A$ in the state $\rho$ is
\[
\langle A \rangle_{\rho}
=
\Trace(\rho A)
\]

Indeed,
\[
\Trace(\rho A)
=
\Trace\left(
\rho \sum_i \lambda_i \ket{\varphi_i}\bra{\varphi_i}
\right)
=
\sum_i \lambda_i \Trace\bigl(\ket{\varphi_i}\bra{\varphi_i}\rho\bigr)
\]
so it is again the weighted sum over the possible measurement outcomes.  % [oai_citation:9‡book_Invitation-to-Quantum-Informatics pages 52 - 59.pdf](sediment://file_00000000b410720abc2d96070c66ef5d)

\subsubsection{Parts of entangled systems and partial trace}

If a bipartite system has density matrix
\[
\rho_{AB} \in \operatorname{End}(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B)
\]
then the state of subsystem $A$ alone is described by the reduced density matrix
\[
\rho_A = \Trace_B(\rho_{AB})
\]
where the partial trace is defined by linear extension of
\[
\Trace_B(S \otimes T) = \Trace(T)S
\]

Similarly,
\[
\rho_B = \Trace_A(\rho_{AB})
\]

For the singlet state
\[
\ket{\psi^-}
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\left(
\ket{0}_A \otimes \ket{1}_B
-
\ket{1}_A \otimes \ket{0}_B
\right)
\]
we have
\[
\rho_{AB} = \ket{\psi^-}\bra{\psi^-}
\]
and
\[
\rho_A = \Trace_B(\rho_{AB}) = \frac{\id_A}{2}
\qquad
\rho_B = \Trace_A(\rho_{AB}) = \frac{\id_B}{2}
\]

Thus each subsystem is maximally mixed although the total state is pure.

Moreover, local measurement statistics are determined entirely by the reduced density matrix.
If Alice measures a projector $P_{\varphi_i}$ on her subsystem, then
\[
\Prob(\lambda_i)
=
\Trace\bigl((P_{\varphi_i} \otimes \id_B)\rho_{AB}\bigr)
=
\Trace\bigl(P_{\varphi_i}\rho_A\bigr)
\]

Hence operations performed only on Bob's subsystem do not alter Alice's local outcome probabilities.
This is the formal reason why entanglement alone does not allow signalling.  % [oai_citation:10‡book_Invitation-to-Quantum-Informatics pages 52 - 59.pdf](sediment://file_00000000b410720abc2d96070c66ef5d)
