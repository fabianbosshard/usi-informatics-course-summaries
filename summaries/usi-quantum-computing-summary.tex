\section{Information is Physical}

\subsection{Stern/Gerlach Experiment}

Measure \emph{magnetic dipole moment} of silver atoms by sending a stream of them through an \emph{inhomogeneous} magnetic field.  Each atom is deflected from the path proportionally to its dipole in the direction of the field gradient.

\subsubsection{Classical expectation}

If we imagine that the moments of the atoms point in random directions, then the classical expectation is that the deflection pattern reaches a maximum in the middle (no deflection) and then symmetrically, monotonically and continuously decreases on the sides.

\paragraph{\textbf{Classical mechanics.}}

Angular momentum (pseudovector):
\[
\vect{L} = \vect{r} \times \vect{p} = \vect{r} \times (m\,\vect{v})
\]

Torque:
\[
\vect{\tau} = \vect{r} \times \vect{F}
\]

Relationship:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tau_L}
\vect{\tau} = \frac{\dif}{\dif t}\,\vect{L}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{\textbf{Classical electrodynamics.}}

A charge $q$ moving in a circle of radius $R$ creates a current:
\[
I = \frac{q}{T}
\quad\text{where}\quad
T = \frac{2\pi R}{v}
\]

Magnetic dipole moment of a current loop:
\[
\vect{\mu} = I\,A\,\hat{n}
\quad\text{where}\quad
A = \pi R^{2}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\hat{n}\ \text{is normal to the area.}
\]

Thus
\[
\vect{\mu}
= \frac{q}{2\pi R/v}\,(\pi R^{2})\,\hat{n}
= \frac{q\,v\,R}{2}\,\hat{n}
\]

Angular momentum:
\[
\vect{L} = m\,\vect{r}\times\vect{v} = m\,R\,v\,\hat{n}
\]

Therefore (the bridge):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mu_gammaL}
\fcolorbox{red}{white}{\(\vect{\mu} = \gamma\,\vect{L}\)}
\end{equation}
with
\[
\gamma = \frac{q}{2m}
\]

\paragraph{\textbf{Magnetic dipole dynamics.}}

Torque:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tau_muB}
\vect{\tau} = \vect{\mu} \times \vect{B}
\end{equation}

Combining \eqref{eq:tau_L}, \eqref{eq:mu_gammaL} and \eqref{eq:tau_muB} gives the precession equation:
\[
\frac{\dif}{\dif t}\,\vect{L} = \gamma\bigl(\vect{L}\times\vect{B}\bigr)
\quad\text{or equivalently}\quad
\frac{\dif}{\dif t}\,\vect{\mu} = \gamma\bigl(\vect{\mu}\times\vect{B}\bigr)
\]
Hence $\frac{\dif\vect{\mu}}{\dif t}\perp\vect{\mu}$, and therefore $\lvert\vect{\mu}\rvert$ is constant (only the direction changes).

Energy:
\[
U = -\,\vect{\mu}\cdot\vect{B}
\]

Force in general is
\[
\vect{F} = \nabla\bigl(\vect{\mu}\cdot\vect{B}\bigr)
\]
and if $\vect{\mu}$ is constant and $\vect{B}\approx [0,0,B_z(z)]$, then
\[
F_{z} \approx \mu_{z}\,\frac{\partial B_{z}}{\partial z}
\]

So, as mentioned above, according to classical physics we would expect to measure
\[
\mu_{z}\in[-\lvert\vect{\mu}\rvert,\,+\lvert\vect{\mu}\rvert]
\]
i.e. a continuous range and thus a continuous vertical smear.

\subsubsection{Observation}

That is, however, not what was observed: there is no detection in (not even close to) the middle, but rather two sharp peaks at equal distances from the middle.

In quantum mechanics, $\vect{L}$ becomes an operator with three components $\hat{L}_{x},\hat{L}_{y},\hat{L}_{z}$ and they do not commute, e.g.
\[
[\hat{L}_{x},\hat{L}_{y}]
:= \hat{L}_{x}\hat{L}_{y}-\hat{L}_{y}\hat{L}_{x}
= \iu \hbar \hat{L}_{z}
\]
This means we cannot simultaneously know all three components exactly.  What is possible is to simultaneously know one component and the total angular momentum operator:
\[
\hat{L}^{2} = \hat{L}_{x}^{2}+\hat{L}_{y}^{2}+\hat{L}_{z}^{2}
\]
$\hat{L}^{2}$ has eigenvalues $\ell(\ell+1)\hbar^{2}$:
\[
\hat{L}^{2}\,|\ell,m_{\ell}\rangle = \ell(\ell+1)\,\hbar^{2}\,|\ell,m_{\ell}\rangle
\]
Projection quantization:
\[
\hat{L}_{z}\,|\ell,m_{\ell}\rangle = m_{\ell}\,\hbar\,|\ell,m_{\ell}\rangle
\]
with $m_{\ell}=-\ell,-\ell+1,\dots,\ell-1,\ell$.

Even when orbital $\ell=0$, particles can still have angular momentum (spin) $\vect{S}$.  It also satisfies $\hat{S}^{2} = \hat{S}_{x}^{2} + \hat{S}_{y}^{2} + \hat{S}_{z}^{2}$ and
\[
\hat{S}^{2}\,|s,m_{s}\rangle = s(s+1)\,\hbar^{2}\,|s,m_{s}\rangle,
\quad
\hat{S}_{z}\,|s,m_{s}\rangle = m_{s}\,\hbar\,|s,m_{s}\rangle
\]
For the electron, for example, $m_{s} = \pm\tfrac{1}{2}$ so $\hat{S}_{z}$ has eigenvalues $\pm\,\hbar/2$.

Magnetic moment in quantum mechanics:
\[
\vect{\mu} = \vect{\mu}_{L} + \vect{\mu}_{S}
\]
where
$\vect{\mu}_{L} = \tfrac{q}{2m}\,\vect{L}$
and
$\vect{\mu}_{S} = g\,\tfrac{q}{2m}\,\vect{S}$
(for the electron: $g \approx 2$).

For silver, the valence electron is in an $s$-orbital ($\ell=0$), so $\vect{\mu}_{L}\approx\vect{0}$.  The remaining contribution is spin: $s=\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $m_{s}=\pm\tfrac{1}{2}$, hence $\mu_{z}$ can take only two values.  This is the reason for the two peaks.

\subsubsection{Repeated measurements}

For a spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ particle the transition probability between ``up'' along two axes separated by an angle $\theta$ is
\[
P = \cos^{2}\frac{\theta}{2}.
\]
The extra factor of $\tfrac{1}{2}$ in the angle appears because spin states are \emph{spinors} (an $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ representation): 
a physical rotation by $\theta$ acts on the state with half the angle in the two-dimensional Hilbert space.

The essential property of waves is that they can be linearly combined.  
Quantum mechanical states have the same property: 
they are elements in a vector space and we can derive probability distributions from them by squaring.  
% For our silver atom, we can write

If, after a $Z$ measurement, we perform an $X$ measurement, then we want to know wether the silver atom is in a state $\ket{x_+}$ or $\ket{x_-}$. 
Both are superpositions:
\[
\ket{x_+} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{z_+} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{z_-}
\qquad
\ket{x_-} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{z_+} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{z_-}
\]

No matter the outcome of the first measurement, the $X$ measurement yields one of both results with equal probability.
Also in the other direction, if we first measure $X$ and then $Z$, we get the same statistics, independent of any measurements before the $X$ measurement.

\subsection{Quantum Key Distribution}\label{sec:quantum-key-distribution}

If a quantum system is measured twice in the same basis, the same result is obtained with certainty. 
However, an intermediate measurement in a different basis disturbs the state and can change the outcome.
So the interactions of a system with its environment become traceable. 
This can be used to detect an eavesdropper in a cryptographic key agreement protocol.

In 1984, Gilles Brassard and Charles Bennett developed the first application of quantum mechanics for cryptographic purposes with such a key agreement protocol (BB84).

Assume Alice and Bob want to agree on a secret key; e.g. for \emph{symmetric encryption} (same key for encryption and decryption).
They can proceed as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Alice repeatedly measures a random bit in a random basis (e.g., $Z$ or $X$) and sends the quantum system to Bob.
  \item Bob measures the received system in a randomly chosen basis ($Z$ or $X$).
\end{enumerate}
  For the bits where they used the same basis, the measurements must be the same, unless there has been an eavesdropper, Eve, measuring the system in a different basis during its transmission from Alice to Bob. 
  Since Eve does not know the true basis, the best thing to do for her is to guess it, so her probability of guessing right is 50\%.
  If, she guesses wrong, the bit Bob recieves will be incorrect with a chance of 50\%, so in total, 25\% of the bits will be wrong.
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Alice and Bob communicate over an \emph{public authenticated classical channel} which basis they used, without sharing the measurement results.
  \item They randomly select some of the shared bits, reveal them publicly and check if they match.
  \item If the error rate for those selected bits is high (around 25\%), Eve is present, and they discard the key.
  \item If the error rate is very low: No eavesdropping detected
  \item They keep the remaining unrevealed bits as the secret key.
\end{enumerate}
